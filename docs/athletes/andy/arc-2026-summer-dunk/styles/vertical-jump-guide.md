# Vertical Jump — Training Style Guide

> **Purpose of this guide.** This document is intended to help coaches — human or AI — design programs using vertical-jump training. It contains all relevant information about the methodology, including text, tables, and diagrams, so a coach reading it can design programs as effectively as possible. Synthesized from **THP Jump Training Ebook** (THPStrength.com) and **The Vertical Jump Bible 2.0** (Kelly Baggett).

A power and reactivity-focused training style for athletes pursuing maximal vertical jump height.

---

## 1. When to use this style

**Goal types this serves:**
- Maximal vertical jump (one-foot or two-foot)
- Dunk capability for basketball
- Block jump / spike jump for volleyball
- Approach jump for any sport requiring elevation
- General lower-body reactive power

**Athlete profile fit:**
- Training age: any (programming branches sharply by tier — see §3)
- Equipment minimum: barbell + rack + plates + open space (15+ ft for plyo work) + a box for depth jumps
- Time required: 3–5 sessions/week, 60–90 min each
- Compatible co-styles: general strength, sport practice, mobility, basketball/volleyball technical work

**Contraindications (do NOT recruit this style if):**
- Active patellar tendinopathy with pain >4 — depth jumps and high-volume reactive work will worsen the condition
- Post-ACL reconstruction <12 weeks — needs return-to-sport phase first
- Severe ankle / knee instability without orthopedic clearance
- Squat <1× bodyweight in a beginner — build base with general strength first; plyometric overload is wasted on athletes who can't yet absorb it

## 2. Mechanism — how this style produces results

Jump height is the velocity of the athlete's center of mass at takeoff. Velocity is the product of how much force they can produce and how fast they can produce it. Vertical-jump training trains both *and* teaches the body to express them together — that's the operational thesis the rules below derive from.

### 2.1 Force–velocity relationship

```
Jump Height = f(Takeoff Velocity)
Takeoff Velocity = f(Force × Rate of Force Development)
Power = Force × Velocity
```

Two levers: increase total force, or increase how fast you produce it. Every exercise selection, periodization choice, and rep scheme serves one or both.

![Force-Velocity Curve](diagrams/force-velocity-curve.svg)

**Four training zones on the curve:**

| Zone | Load Range | Key Exercises | What It Trains |
|------|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| Max Strength | >80% 1RM | Back squat, front squat, deadlift | Raw force production capacity |
| Strength-Speed | 50–80% 1RM | Power cleans, snatches, speed squats | Force production at moderate speeds |
| Speed-Strength | 15–50% 1RM | Jump squats, weighted jumps | Velocity under moderate load |
| Max Velocity | 0–15% load | Depth jumps, bounds, sprints, approach jumps | Maximum speed of force expression |

**Key insight:** The vertical jump sits at the peak of the power curve — where force × velocity is maximized. Pure strength is insufficient; pure speed is insufficient. An effective program develops both sides and teaches the body to express them together.

### 2.2 Explosive strength deficit (ESD)

The gap between an athlete's maximum force capacity (a slow squat) and the force they express in a fast movement (a jump).

![Explosive Strength Deficit](diagrams/explosive-strength-deficit.svg)

Per Zatsiorsky (cited in VJB), athletes typically express only ~50% of their maximum force during jump takeoffs. The rest is "left on the table" because the movement happens too fast to develop full force.

**Measuring the ESD:** Compare the squat-to-bodyweight ratio against the vertical jump. If squat goes up but VJ stays flat, the deficit is growing. If VJ goes up while squat holds steady, the deficit is shrinking.

**Programming implication:** Don't let the ESD grow indefinitely. Over a full training arc (12–20 weeks), squat gains and VJ gains should both trend up.

**Three ways to close the deficit:**

1. **Increase max force** — Heavy squats. Raises the ceiling.
2. **Improve rate of force development** — Jump squats, Olympic lifts, speed squats. Trains the nervous system to fire faster.
3. **Train reactivity / SSC** — Depth jumps, bounds, approach jumps. Overloads the elastic component.

This is what tells the agent that the program must address all three. Strength alone widens the deficit; plyometric work alone has no ceiling to close.

### 2.3 Stretch-shortening cycle (SSC)

The mechanism that makes plyometric training work. When a muscle is rapidly stretched (eccentric) then immediately contracts (concentric), it produces more force than a concentric-only contraction — due to elastic energy stored in tendons plus the stretch reflex.

**Two types:**

| Type | Ground Contact Time | Examples | What It Trains |
|------|-------------------|----------|----------------|
| Short SSC | <250 ms | Ankle jumps, depth jumps, bounds | Tendon stiffness, reactive ability |
| Long SSC | >250 ms | Countermovement jumps, approach jumps | Muscular force + elastic rebound |

**The vertical jump uses both.** The approach + penultimate step is long SSC; the final takeoff (especially one-foot) is short SSC. Training must address both.

**Three types of plyometric overload (THP):**

1. **Resistive** — Increase gravitational / height component. Higher box for depth jumps, weighted vests.
2. **Spatial** — Manipulate ROM or starting position. Deeper countermovement, different approach angles.
3. **Temporal** — Decrease ground contact time. Faster operating rate.

Programming note: overload only one variable at a time. Increasing box height *and* demanding shorter contact creates contradictory demands.

**Plyometric volume guidelines (both sources agree):**
- Finish plyo work feeling fresh or fresher than at the start
- The effectiveness comes from neural connections — quality over quantity
- ~20 working sets per session is the ceiling
- Depth jump cycles: 4×5, 2–3×/week, 2–3 weeks then deload
- High-box depth jumps (true shock methods): restrict to 2–3 week concentrated blocks

## 3. Athlete assessment

Before programming, assess the athlete. This determines where on the force–velocity curve to focus training.

![Assessment Flowchart](diagrams/assessment-flowchart.svg)

### Step 1: Strength threshold check

Both sources agree on a minimum strength threshold before anything else matters:

**Squat ≥ 1.5× bodyweight** is the qualifying standard. Below this, the athlete focuses almost exclusively on basic leg strength + low-level plyometrics for coordination.

VJB: *"Beginners without a solid strength base should ALWAYS focus on basic leg strengthening exercises and low level plyometrics."*

### Step 2: Reactive deficit test

For athletes above the strength threshold, compare:
- **Standing countermovement vertical jump** (best of 3–5 attempts)
- **Bounce depth jump** from an 18-inch box (best of 3–5 attempts)

| Result | Diagnosis | Training Focus |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Standing VJ ≥ Bounce VJ | Strength dominant — expresses force well but lacks reactive ability | Emphasize plyometrics, jump squats, explosive work |
| Bounce VJ > Standing VJ | Speed/plyo dominant — efficient at expressing force, needs more raw strength | Drive squat higher, consider hypertrophy, maintain plyo efficiency |
| Roughly equal | Balanced | Concurrent development of both qualities |

### Step 3: Structural and profile assessment

VJB identifies broader athlete archetypes:

- **Stronger-than-fast** — thicker builds, larger calves/ankles, shorter legs, quad dominant, less "quick" unloaded. Respond well to explosive training; shouldn't neglect strength.
- **Faster-than-strong** — lean builds, higher calf insertions, glute dominant, inherently "quick." Respond well to strength + hypertrophy.

### Squat-to-bodyweight programming guide

| Ratio | Level | Programming Approach |
|-------|-------|---------------------|
| <1.5× BW | Beginner | Novice linear progression (squat 3×5, add weight each session) |
| 1.5–2.0× BW | Intermediate | Conjugate periodization, begin emphasizing power |
| 2.0–2.5× BW | Advanced | Shift emphasis toward power expression and reactivity |
| >2.5× BW | Elite | Diminishing returns on strength; focus on RFD, plyometrics, technique |

### In-block monitoring (capture each session)

- Top set load on the day's primary lift
- Plyometric "feel" — fresh, neutral, fatigued (informs whether to cap volume early)
- Best jump height of the session (use as the training max, not a competitive max)
- Athlete's reported readiness (1–10) and any pain flags

## 4. Session structure

Both THP and VJB agree on session ordering.

![Session Structure](diagrams/session-structure.svg)

**The rule: fastest movements first, while neurally fresh.**

1. **Speed/Plyometric work** — Depth jumps, bounds, tuck jumps, approach jumps. Maximum intent, low volume, full recovery between sets.
2. **Power/Ballistic work** — Jump squats (15–30% of max squat), hang cleans, hang snatches. Moderate load, high velocity.
3. **Strength work** — Back squat, front squat, deadlift. Heavy loads, controlled tempo, full rest.
4. **Accessories/Mobility** — Bulgarian split squats, RDLs, hip thrusts, calf raises, core, flexibility.

**Rest intervals:**
- Plyometrics: 1–2 min between sets
- Power movements: 3–4 min
- Heavy strength work: 4–5 min (THP: non-negotiable for large weights)
- Accessories: 1–2 min

**Why this order matters:**
- Plyometric movements require maximal neural output; doing them after squats degrades quality
- The nervous system is most excitable at session start
- Heavy strength creates fatigue that compromises explosive intent
- Different exercise types create different fatigue traces (THP's fatigue specificity); proper sequencing avoids superimposition

### Post-activation potentiation (PAP)

VJB describes "stimulation methods" — heavy → explosive sequencing for temporary performance boost:

- Heavy squat (5 reps at 85%) → rest 5 min → jump squats (6–8 reps at 20%)
- Weighted jump (10–20% BW) × 5 → rest 3 min → unloaded jump × 3
- Depth jump × 5 → rest 3 min → running jump for max height × 3

Most effective for intermediate athletes during peaking phases. Use sparingly — restrict to 2–3 week cycles.

### Typical duration & frequency

- 60–90 minutes per session
- 2–4 sessions per week (most athletes do 3)
- Higher frequencies (5+) only for advanced athletes with very high recovery capacity

## 5. Exercise selection

The full filtered catalog with style annotations is in `exercise-selection.md`. This section is the agent's quick-reference: canonical primaries, allowed accessories, substitutions, and disallowed.

### Primary strength exercises

| Exercise | Purpose | Programming Notes |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| Back Squat | Primary lower body strength builder | Full depth, controlled negative, accelerate up. 3–5 sets of 1–6 reps. |
| Front Squat | Quad emphasis, upright torso | Good alternative for athletes with back issues. Same set/rep ranges. |
| Bulgarian Split Squat | Unilateral strength, hip stability | 2–3 sets of 6–8 per side. Addresses imbalances. |
| Romanian Deadlift | Posterior chain, hamstrings | 3 sets of 6–10. Supports hip extension for jump takeoff. |
| Barbell Hip Thrust | Glute activation, hip extension | 3 sets of 6–10. Directly trains primary jumping muscles. |
| Calf Raise | Ankle extension strength | 2–3 sets of 15–20. Higher reps because calves are endurance-oriented. |

### Power / ballistic exercises

| Exercise | Purpose | Programming Notes |
|----------|---------|-------------------|
| Jump Squat | Primary ballistic for VJ | 15–30% of max squat. 3–5 sets of 3–5 reps. Pause and reset each rep. |
| Hang Power Clean | Triple extension, RFD | 3–5 sets of 3–5 reps. Technique matters — don't chase weight at the expense of speed. |
| Hang Power Snatch | Full-body explosiveness | 3–5 sets of 3 reps. Lighter than cleans, higher bar speed. |

### Plyometric exercises

| Exercise | Intensity | Purpose | Programming Notes |
|----------|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Ankle Jumps | Low | Short SSC, tendon stiffness | 4 sets of 8. Minimize ground contact. |
| Tuck Jumps | Low-Med | Coordination, reactive ability | 4 sets of 8. Drive knees up maximally. |
| Lateral Barrier Jumps | Low-Med | Lateral reactive ability | 4 sets of 8 over mid-shin obstacle. |
| Low Box Depth Jumps | Med | Teach landing mechanics | 2–4 sets of 5. Box height where athlete can comfortably rebound. |
| Depth Jumps (optimal) | High | Maximum reactive overload | 2–4 sets of 5. Find optimal box height (see below). Use sparingly. |
| Bounds / Alternate Leg Bounds | Med-High | Horizontal → vertical power transfer | 2–4 sets of 25–30 yards. |

### Substitution map

| If unavailable / contraindicated | Substitute | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Back squat (low-back issues) | Front squat or SSB squat | Reduces shear on lumbar, preserves quad stimulus |
| Hang power clean (wrist injury) | Trap-bar jump or KB swing | Triple extension stimulus without front-rack catch |
| Depth jump (patellar tendinopathy) | Ankle jumps + low-box approach jumps | Maintains reactive work without high impact load |
| Olympic lifts (technical learning curve) | Speed squats + jump squats | Trains RFD without months of technique acquisition |

### Disallowed in this style

| Exercise | Reason for exclusion |
|---|---|
| Slow tempo squats (3-1-3 or longer) as a primary | Wastes RFD-training opportunity; only valid as accessory |
| Long-distance running | Antagonistic to power adaptations; degrades fast-twitch |
| Static stretching pre-jump session | Acutely reduces force output; do dynamic warm-up instead |

### Finding optimal depth jump box height (VJB)

Start at 12 inches. Test bounce jump height. Increase by 6 inches at a time. The optimal height is where the athlete achieves their highest rebound. If performance drops at the next height, the previous was optimal. If the athlete feels "heavy" or collapses on landing, the box is too high.

## 6. Periodization

![Conjugate Periodization](diagrams/conjugate-periodization.svg)

**Model used:** Conjugate sequence for intermediate and advanced athletes. Linear concurrent for beginners.

**Why this model fits this style:** Vertical jump requires multiple qualities (strength, RFD, reactivity) that are interdependent. Sequential periodization detrains the qualities you're not currently emphasizing. Conjugate sequence trains all of them all the time, just with shifting emphasis.

### Periodization models in this style's vocabulary

- **Sequential** — One quality at a time. Works for beginners; risks losing previously developed qualities at higher levels.
- **Concurrent** — All qualities trained equally, all the time. Works for novices (VJB's beginner template). Limitation: eventually one quality needs more focus.
- **Conjugate sequence** — All qualities trained simultaneously, with a shifting spotlight. **THP's primary method, VJB's recommendation for intermediate+.**

### Conjugate sequence: how it works

**Core principle:** Always train all motor qualities, but vary the emphasis per phase. Maintain non-emphasized qualities at ~1/3 of their building volume (VJB rule).

**THP's 1–2 target rule:** Focus on 1–2 motor abilities per mesocycle. 70–80% of work in that block serves those targets.

**Phase structure (typical 10–14 week arc):**

| Phase | Duration | Emphasis | Strength Volume | Power Volume | Reactive Volume |
|-------|----------|----------|----------------|--------------|----------------|
| I: Foundation | 3–4 wks | Strength | HIGH (5×5, 5×6) | LOW (2×8 jump squats) | LOW (2×10 low box) |
| II: Build | 2–3 wks | Power/RFD | MAINTAIN (6×1 @ 90%) | HIGH (6×5 jump squats) | MEDIUM (2×10 med box) |
| III: Intensify | 2–3 wks | Reactivity | LOW (3×3 maintenance) | MAINTAIN (2×5 JS) | HIGH (4×5 high box depth jumps) |
| IV: Realize | 1–2 wks | Peak/Test | MINIMAL (2×5 half squat) | LOW (2×5 JS) | LOW (2×10 low box) |

### Delayed transformation

The key insight that makes periodization work. Both sources emphasize that gains from a training phase don't manifest *during* that phase — they manifest *afterward*, during reduced loading.

- Hard training induces fatigue that masks adaptation
- A period of relatively easy exercise is needed to realize previous hard training (THP: "delayed transformation")
- Phase IV volume drops dramatically — not because you're lazy, but so the body can express what it built in Phases I–III
- VJB calls this "concentrated loading" — train hard for several weeks, then recover and reap delayed benefits

### Within-block progression rules

- **Bump load** when all prescribed sets are completed with good form (primary for strength)
- **Hold load** if reps are clean but RPE has crept up; another week at the same load consolidates the adaptation
- **Back off** if reps slow visibly or RPE jumps 1+; reduce weight 10% and rebuild
- **Deload every 3–4 weeks** — reduce volume 30–50%, intensity moderate. VJB: drop weights 10%, drop sets by 1–2 per exercise.

### Between-block progression rules

- Shift emphasis (strength → power → reactive → peak)
- Introduce new exercises (front squat → back squat, swap plyo variations) to avoid accommodation
- Increase intensity while managing volume
- Test and re-assess (VJ test, squat test) at end of each block

### Practical periodization rules

1. **Strength phases longer than speed phases** — VJB recommends speed phases be 50–66% the length of strength phases.
2. **Don't overcomplicate rep schemes** — VJB: simple, repeatable. THP: rotate within a block (3×3, 3×8, 3×15, 4×2) but keep exercises fixed.
3. **Fixed exercises per block, varied load** — change load not exercises within a mesocycle. Swap exercises between blocks.
4. **The rule of 60%** — light day volume is ~60% of max day in the same microcycle.
5. **Back-to-back sessions should not hit the same muscle groups** — proper rotation is essential when training 5–6×/week.

## 7. Sample programs

### Novice template (VJB-inspired)

> For athletes with squat <1.5× BW. Train 3 days per week (Mon/Wed/Fri or Tue/Thu/Sat).

```
Session (same each day):

A: Speed/Plyo (pick 2, 4 sets of 5–8 reps each)
   - Tuck jump, ankle jump, low box depth jump, lateral barrier jump

B: Power (pick 1, 3 sets of 5)
   - Jump squat @ 20% of squat max
   - Hang power clean
   - Hang power snatch

C: Strength
   - Squat: 3×5 (add 10 lb when all 3 sets completed with perfect form)
   - RDL or barbell hip thrust: 2×8–10
   - Calf raise: 3×15–20
   - Bench/military press (alternate): 3×5 (same progression as squat)
   - Pull-ups: 3 sets to failure (add weight when total reps exceed 24)
```

**Progression:** Add weight each session you complete all prescribed sets with good form. Stalled 1+ week → back-cycle: reduce weight 10%, work back up.

**Expected gains:** 6+ inches on VJ in first 1–2 months if novice. 100+ lb squat increase in 6 months.

### Intermediate strength-focused (VJB-inspired)

> For athletes with squat ≥1.5× BW who need more strength. 4 days per week.

```
Week 1–3 rotation:

Monday (Upper #1):
  A: Standing/running VJ practice: 10–15 total reps
     Depth jumps or bounds: 2×5 (add 1 set/week to 4 sets)
  B: Bench press: follow 3-week wave (see below)
     Incline DB press: 2×6–10
     Cable row: 3×6–10

Tuesday (Lower #1):
  A: Low plyos (ankle jump, tuck jump, barrier jump): 4×8 (pick 2)
  B: Hang clean or jump squat: follow rep wave
     Squat: follow 3-week wave
     RDL/hip thrust: 3×6–8
     Calf raise: 2×20

Thursday (Upper #2):
  A: Standing/running VJ practice: 10–15 reps
     Depth jumps or bounds: 2×5 → 4×5
  B: Military press: follow wave
     Pull-ups: 3 sets max reps
     DB bench: 3×8–10

Saturday (Lower #2):
  A: Low plyos: 4×8 (pick 2)
  B: Hang snatch or jump squat: follow wave
     Deadlift or front squat: follow wave
     Bulgarian split squat: 2–3×6–8/side
     RDL/hip thrust: 3×6–8
     Calf raise: 2×20

3-Week Rep Wave (for squat, deadlift, bench, military, cleans, snatches):
  Week 1: 3×5, 1×max reps @ 65%, 75%, 85%, 85%
  Week 2: 3×3, 1×max reps @ 70%, 80%, 90%, 90%
  Week 3: 1×5, 2×3, 1×max reps @ 75%, 85%, 95%, 95%
  Week 4 (deload): 3×3 with Week 1 weight, eliminate Saturday lower workout, test VJ
```

### Intermediate explosive-focused (VJB-inspired)

> For athletes who are strong enough but need to close the explosive strength deficit.

```
Monday:
  Tuck/ankle/barrier jump: 4×8
  Hang clean or jump squat: 4×3–5 (15–20% squat max for JS)
  Complex with: Depth jump onto box: 4×5
  Squat: 8×1 @ 85–90%
  RDL/hip thrust: 2×6–10

Wednesday:
  Tuck/ankle/barrier jump: 4×8
  Depth jump: 4×5
  Jump squat: 4×3 @ 15–20%
  Speed squat: 6×2 @ 60–70%
  Bulgarian split squat: 3×6–8

Friday:
  Tuck/ankle/barrier jump: 4×8
  Hang clean or snatch: 4×3
  Depth jump: 2×5
  Squat: 5×3 @ 80–85%
  Calf raise: 3×20
```

### Conjugate sequence 3-exercise example (VJB)

A minimal but complete conjugate cycle using only squat, jump squat, and depth jump:

```
Phase I — Strength Focus (3 weeks, 2 sessions/week):
  Session A: Depth jump 2×10 low box | Jump squat 2×8 @ 15% | Squat 5×6
  Session B: Depth jump 2×10 low box | Jump squat 2×8 @ 15% | Squat 8×1 @ 90%

Phase II — Power Focus (2 weeks, 2 sessions/week):
  Session A: Depth jump 2×10 med box | Jump squat 6×5 @ 15–30% (wave up and down) | Squat 6×1 @ 90%
  Session B: Depth jump 2×10 med box | Jump squat 6×5 @ 15–30% | Speed squat 6×2 @ 60%

Phase III — Reactive Focus (2 weeks, 3 sessions/week):
  Session A: Depth jump 4×5 high box | Jump squat 2×5 @ 15% | Half squat 6×1–3 @ 85%
  Session B: Depth jump 4×5 med box (recovery session)
  Session C: Depth jump 4×5 high box | Jump squat 2×5 @ 15% | Squat 3×3 w/ 5RM

Phase IV — Peak (2 weeks, 2 sessions/week):
  Session A: Depth jump 2×10 low box | Half squat 2×5 → Jump squat 2×5 (complex) | 15%
  Session B: Jumps for max height — TEST
```

## 8. Technique

Training alone won't maximize the vertical jump. Technique — particularly the approach and penultimate step — can add 4–6 inches.

### Two variables that determine jump potential

1. **How low you are** — Position of your center of mass at touchdown of the plant foot. Lower = longer time to apply force = more impulse. But too low = leg buckling and poor leverage.
2. **How fast you are** — Velocity of your center of mass at touchdown. Achieved through the run-up and penultimate step. Speed allows the athlete to take advantage of the SSC.

Being low and fast gives **potential** to jump high, but the underlying physiology must handle the loading.

### Two-foot jump mechanics

Key components: the approach, the penultimate step, the arm swing, the plant foot, the block foot, the takeoff.

**The approach (run-up):**
- Purpose: develop horizontal speed and lower center of gravity
- Rhythm should start slow and build progressively
- Most common error: not lowering enough during lead-up steps
- Never slow down mid-approach — maintain or accelerate
- A slight curve in the approach helps with lateral/rotational advantage and lowers the athlete more naturally

**The penultimate step:**
- The most important and hardest-to-master part of the approach (THP)
- Second-to-last step before takeoff
- Where the athlete lowers their center of mass the most and achieves highest horizontal velocity
- Foot contacts slightly in front of hips
- Favor a long, fluid stride — not short and jerky
- As the penultimate foot leaves the ground, kick the plant leg out relatively straight
- Converts horizontal momentum to vertical through braking forces that load the quadriceps
- Penultimate stride length is less important than being low and fast

**The arm swing:**
- Underrated contributor to jump height
- Benefits: raises center of mass at takeoff AND increases ground reaction forces (Newton's 3rd law)
- Timing is critical: arms begin moving forward and upward as the block foot moves forward
- As hands pass in front of hips, the block foot should contact the ground

### One-foot jump differences

- Favors greater ankle and hip extensor (glute/calf) activation
- Knee and ankle extensors can't contribute as much positive energy compared to two-foot jump
- Structural factors matter more: leg length, hip width, tendon stiffness
- Training focus: keep body fat low, establish glute dominance, strengthen quads and calves for force absorption, use shorter-response plyometric variations

## 9. Recovery & nutrition

Universal recovery floors (sleep ≥7h, protein 0.8–1g/lb, hydration, basic nutrition) live in `concepts/recovery.md`. This section adds vertical-jump-specific demands on top.

### Style-specific recovery demands

- **CNS recovery between high-CNS sessions** — depth jumps, max-effort jumps, and heavy squats all draw from the same neural budget. Schedule ≥48h between high-CNS sessions.
- **Tendon recovery is the rate-limiter for plyo blocks** — patellar and Achilles tendons need 48–72h between high-volume reactive work. Volume creep here is the most common cause of mid-block stalls.
- **Approach jumps are CNS-expensive even though they look "fun"** — count them in the session's reactive volume budget.

### Style-specific nutritional priorities

- **Body fat over bodyweight** — VJB challenges "stay light" thinking. A bigger muscle is potentially a stronger muscle and usually generates more than enough additional force to offset extra weight. Focus on body fat %, not absolute weight.
- **Exception: one-foot jumping responds less well to weight gain** — more about leverage than raw strength. One-foot jumpers should err lighter.
- **Don't restrict too aggressively during a strength block** — no more than 2 lb/week if cutting; aggressive deficits compromise force production.

### Training frequency

- Most athletes need 3–4 hours total quality training per week (excluding warm-ups)
- 2–3 sessions/week is sufficient for most; some athletes do well on 1
- If sport practice exceeds 2–3 hr/week, reduce dedicated training to 1–2 sessions
- **In-season:** maintenance mode. 1–2 sessions per week to retain strength + quality. THP: two 30–40 min heavy sessions per week is usually enough to retain strength.
- Recovery workouts use DIFFERENT muscle groups than the main workout, or are very low intensity

## 10. Coach's notes

Tacit knowledge worth passing on that doesn't fit cleanly in the sections above:

- **Track training maxes, not competitive maxes.** There's typically a 10% gap between a relaxed/unmotivated jump (training max) and a competitive max (high arousal, full warm-up). Programming progression off competitive maxes is what causes "I never PR anymore" frustration. Use the boring number — that's the one that compares apples to apples week to week.
- **VJ gains come in spurts, then stagnate.** "Jump gains tend to come in spurts, followed by weeks and months of stagnation." Don't panic-tweak the program during a stagnation period — usually it resolves with one more block of patient loading. The usual mistake is to overhaul the program two weeks before the breakthrough that was about to happen.
- **The "lazy man's activity" framing is real.** VJB explicitly says proper VJ training is *less* volume than people expect. If you're writing programs that have the athlete in the gym 10 hours a week, you're probably overdoing it. The neural quality matters more than the total work.
- **Watch the squat-to-VJ ratio drift.** This is the single best in-block diagnostic for whether the program is working as a whole. Strength up, jump flat = ESD widening, shift to power/reactive emphasis. Jump up, strength flat = good news, you're closing the deficit; consider whether to bring strength back into emphasis.
- **Past 2.5× BW squat, more strength has diminishing returns.** Pivot harder to RFD and reactivity. The "just keep adding weight" instinct breaks down here.
- **Tendinopathy is the silent killer of plyo blocks.** Patellar pain creeping from 1 → 2 → 3 over 3 weeks is the signal to deload, not push through. Athletes who report "it just feels stiff in the morning" before a high-box depth jump session almost always end up with a 2-week setback if you don't pull volume immediately.
- **Approach mechanics deserve actual coaching, not just "do approach jumps."** The penultimate step alone is worth 4–6 inches. Skipping technique work because "the program already has plyos" leaves obvious money on the table.
- **Beginners can grow VJ on almost anything.** Don't confuse beginner gains with the program being optimal. The real test of programming is on intermediate athletes who've already harvested the easy gains.
- **In-season, don't try to peak; try to hold.** The biggest in-season mistake is treating it like an off-season micro-cycle. Maintenance mode is the goal — anything ambitious has a high chance of injury or fatigue.

## 11. Programming decision tree

The runbook a planning agent follows when designing a VJ block.

### Step 1 — Classify the athlete

- **Inputs:** athlete profile (age, training history) + assessment results from §3
- **Outputs:** tier label (Beginner / Intermediate / Advanced / Elite) + dominance label (Strength-dominant / Speed-dominant / Balanced)

### Step 2 — Pick the periodization model

| Tier | Model |
|---|---|
| Beginner (<1.5× BW squat, <1 yr training) | Linear concurrent. Same workout 3×/week with progressive overload. |
| Intermediate (1.5–2.0× BW, 1–3 yrs) | Conjugate sequence with 3–4 week blocks. Shift emphasis between strength and power/reactive phases. |
| Advanced (>2.0× BW, 3+ yrs) | Conjugate sequence with concentrated loading. Longer strength blocks (4–6 wks), shorter peaking blocks (2–3 wks). |

### Step 3 — Build the session template

Follow the A→B→C→D ordering from §4: Plyos → Power → Strength → Accessories.

Assign exercises from §5 based on:
- Current phase emphasis (what gets more volume per the §6 phase table)
- Athlete's weak point per §3 (strength-dominant gets more plyos; speed-dominant gets more strength)
- Available equipment
- Injury constraints (use §5 substitution map)

### Step 4 — Set volume and intensity

| Component | Building Volume | Maintenance Volume |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|
| Strength (squat etc.) | 3–5 sets × 3–6 reps, 2–3×/week | 2–3 sets × 3–5 reps, 1–2×/week |
| Power (jump squat etc.) | 4–6 sets × 3–5 reps, 2–3×/week | 2 sets × 5 reps, 1–2×/week |
| Plyometrics | 3–4 sets × 5–8 reps, 2–3×/week | 2 sets × 8–10 reps from low box, 1–2×/week |
| Approach/technique | 10–20 jumps, 2–3×/week | 5–10 jumps, 1×/week |

### Step 5 — Define progression

**Within a block:**
- Weight increase when all sets completed with good form (primary for strength)
- Rep scheme rotation per THP (rotate 3×3, 3×8, 3×15, 4×2; try to beat last PR on each scheme)
- Box height progression (only when athlete cleanly rebounds from current height)
- Approach speed (gradually increase as mechanics solidify)

**Between blocks:**
- Shift emphasis (strength → power → reactive → peak)
- Introduce new exercises (swap front squat for back squat, change plyo variations)
- Increase intensity while managing volume
- Test and re-assess (VJ test, squat test at end of each block)

## 12. Common mistakes

1. **Too much variety.** "The magic isn't in how many exercises you do, it's how consistently you can progress in your chosen exercises over time." Use a small handful of basics and progress them.
2. **Too much volume.** Proper VJ training is "a lazy man's activity compared to most sports." 3–4 hours of quality training per week is plenty.
3. **Skipping the strength phase.** Below 1.5× BW squat, focus on strength. Below 2.0× BW, strength remains a significant component.
4. **Grinding plyometrics.** If reps are slow or sloppy, stop the set. Quality over quantity. Finish plyo work feeling fresh.
5. **Expecting linear VJ gains.** "Jump gains tend to come in spurts, followed by weeks and months of stagnation." Track training maxes (relaxed, unmotivated jumps), not competitive maxes.
6. **Training too hard in-season.** In-season priority is maintaining muscle and strength while managing fatigue. 1–2 maintenance sessions per week, keep bodyweight stable.
7. **Ignoring technique.** The penultimate step alone can add inches. Always include some approach jump practice.
8. **Over-relying on competitive maxes.** Up to 10% difference between competitive and training max — don't expect to match PRs every session.
9. **Stacking high-CNS sessions back-to-back.** Depth jumps the day after heavy squats produces fatigued plyos and muddied training stimulus.
10. **Pushing through tendon pain.** The instinct to "work through stiffness" is what turns a 1-week deload into a 6-week setback.

## 13. Style interactions

Vertical-jump training rarely runs alone — it's almost always combined with general strength, sport practice, and mobility. The combinations have hard rules.

**Day-pairing rules:**
- **Heavy squat day must precede jump day by ≥48h.** Same-day or next-day stacking degrades plyo quality and risks tendon injury. Acceptable order: jump day → 48h rest → heavy squat → 48h rest → jump day.
- **Depth jumps and sprints share a CNS budget** — limit to one per session, ideally one per day.
- **Approach jumps stack as plyos, not as skill work.** Don't add them on top of an already-loaded plyo session.

**Recovery overlap with other styles:**
- General strength training adds to the squat volume already in the VJ program — coordinate so total weekly squat volume doesn't exceed what the athlete can recover from.
- Sport practice (basketball, volleyball) often contains incidental plyo work — count it against the weekly plyo budget. A 2-hour basketball practice can easily contain 100+ jumps.
- Sprint training competes for the CNS budget. If sprinting is also being trained, drop dedicated VJ to 2 sessions/week.

**Calorie / load conflicts:**
- Adds ~150–250 kcal training-day demand on top of base — coordinate with the cut/maintain/bulk phase.
- During a cut, reduce VJ volume by ~20% and prioritize strength maintenance over plyo progression.

**Equipment / facility conflicts:**
- Needs platform + clear overhead space for jumps + a depth-jump box. Limits where this style can be programmed.
- Olympic lifting equipment doubles for VJ power work — pairs efficiently in a powerlifting/Oly gym.

**Compatible co-styles:**
- General strength (recommended — provides the squat base VJ training builds on)
- Mobility (recommended — hip mobility and ankle dorsiflexion both gate depth jump quality)
- Basketball / volleyball / track jumps (the sport this style serves)

**Incompatible co-styles:**
- High-volume endurance training (long-distance running, cycling >5 hr/week) — antagonistic adaptations, degrades fast-twitch
- Concurrent maximum-strength powerlifting peak (different recovery curves; pick one to peak)
