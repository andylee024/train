# Session Design Rules

> How to structure a single training session: exercise ordering, volume management, warm-up, and deload protocols. These rules apply across all training styles.

## Exercise Ordering

The nervous system fatigues before muscles do. Exercises that require the most neural output — speed, coordination, max intent — must come first.

### Order of Operations

1. **Warm-up / Activation** (prehab role) — 5-10 minutes. Mobility drills, band work, light movement prep specific to the session's focus.

2. **Speed / Plyometrics** (reactive quality) — When present in a session, these come FIRST after warm-up. Plyos require max intent to be effective. A depth jump at 80% effort is useless. Ground contacts: 40-100 per session depending on intensity tier.

3. **Power / Olympic Lifts** (strength-speed quality) — Cleans, snatches, speed squats. These require coordination and bar speed. They don't work when fatigued.

4. **Strength** (max-strength quality) — Heavy compounds. These still work when somewhat fatigued because the movement is simpler and the load is the stimulus, not the speed.

5. **Accessories** (accessory role) — Isolation and assistance work. Order doesn't matter much. Superset to save time.

6. **Prehab / Mobility** — Can bookend the session. Stretching is fine at the end. PNF work benefits from warm muscles.

### Why This Order Matters

A fatigued nervous system cannot produce max-intent movement. If you squat heavy first, your subsequent depth jumps will be slower and less effective — you're training submaximal reactivity, which defeats the purpose. But squatting after plyos still builds strength because the load (not the speed) is what matters for strength adaptation.

## Volume Management

### By Role

| Role | Sets per Session | Sets per Week | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary | 3-5 working sets | 8-15 | The main driver. Quality over quantity. |
| Secondary | 2-4 sets | 6-12 | Supports the primary. Don't chase PRs here. |
| Accessory | 2-3 sets | 4-9 | Fill gaps. Keep it efficient. |
| Prehab | 1-2 sets | Varies | Non-fatiguing. Don't count toward volume. |

### By Training Quality

| Quality | Reps per Set | Rest Between Sets | Total Weekly Volume |
|---|---|---|---|
| Max Strength | 1-5 | 2:30 - 4:00 | 15-25 working sets (compound) |
| Strength-Speed | 2-3 | 2:00 - 3:00 | 10-18 working sets |
| Speed-Strength | 3-5 | 1:30 - 2:30 | 8-15 working sets |
| Reactive | 3-5 contacts | 2:00 - 3:00 | 40-100 ground contacts/session |
| Skill | Varies | Full recovery | Practice until quality drops |

### Ground Contact Budgets (Plyometrics)

Plyo volume is measured in ground contacts, not sets. One depth jump = 1 contact. One set of 5 bounds = 5 contacts.

| Athlete Tier | Per Session | Per Week | Max Intensity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beginner (plyo) | 40-60 | 80-120 | Medium |
| Intermediate | 60-100 | 120-200 | High |
| Advanced | 80-120 | 160-250 | Max |

Intensity matters more than volume. 10 max-intent depth jumps > 50 lazy tuck jumps.

## Warm-Up Structure

A good warm-up activates the muscles and movement patterns you're about to train without creating fatigue.

1. **General** (2-3 min): Light jog, jump rope, or bike. Just raise body temperature.
2. **Mobility** (3-5 min): Dynamic stretches and drills specific to today's movements. Hip openers before squat day. Shoulder circles before press day.
3. **Activation** (2-3 min): Band work, bodyweight rehearsal of the movement pattern. Banded walks before lower body. Push-ups before bench.
4. **Ramp sets** (2-4 sets): Progressively heavier sets of the first exercise. Don't count these as working sets.

## Deload Protocols

### Standard Deload (every 3-5 weeks)

- Volume: 60% of peak week
- Intensity: 75-80% of peak week
- Keep all exercises in the program (maintain motor patterns)
- Cut accessory work first, then secondary, then reduce primary volume
- Do NOT cut intensity on primary lifts — just reduce sets

### Taper (final 1-2 weeks before a test/competition)

- Volume: 40-50% of peak
- Intensity: maintain or slightly increase (sharpen the nervous system)
- Cut all accessory work
- Keep only primary lifts + sport-specific practice
- Extra sleep and nutrition focus

### Signs You Need an Unplanned Deload

- Weights that felt moderate now feel heavy at the same RPE
- Sleep quality has degraded for 3+ consecutive nights
- Motivation to train has dropped for 5+ consecutive days
- Nagging joint/tendon pain that wasn't there before
- Performance on a key lift drops >5% without explanation

## Contrast / Complex Training (PAP)

Post-activation potentiation: a heavy strength exercise immediately followed (after 2-3 min rest) by an explosive movement targeting the same pattern.

| Heavy Exercise | → Rest 2-3 min → | Explosive Movement |
|---|---|---|
| Back Squat 3×3 @ 85% | | Box Jump 3×5 |
| Barbell Hip Thrust 3×5 | | Broad Jump 3×3 |
| Bench Press 3×3 @ 85% | | Med Ball Push 3×5 |
| Hang Clean 3×3 | | Depth Jump 3×3 |

**When to introduce:** Block 2 (power conversion) and beyond. Not for beginners or Block 1 (the nervous system isn't primed yet).

## Injury Modification Rules

When an athlete has an active injury, modify the affected exercises rather than dropping the movement pattern entirely.

| Injury | Avoid | Replace With |
|---|---|---|
| Shoulder (overhead) | Barbell OHP, behind-neck press | Floor press, neutral grip DB press, landmine press |
| Wrist (De Quervain's) | Front rack clean grip, heavy wrist extension | Straps on all pulls, hang snatch over clean, hook grip |
| Knee (anterior) | Deep squat with forward lean | Box squat, belt squat, reverse sled |
| Low back (acute) | Axial loading, deadlifts | Belt squat, hip thrust, leg press |
| Ankle (sprain) | Impact plyos | Seated jumps, upper body focus, pool work |

The goal is to maintain the training stimulus for the unaffected patterns while the injury heals. Never train through sharp pain.
