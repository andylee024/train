# Periodization Models

> How to sequence training emphasis over time. Each model has trade-offs — the athlete's tier, goal, and timeline determine which model fits.

## Models

### Linear Periodization

Phases run sequentially: hypertrophy → strength → power → peaking. Each phase trains one quality predominantly.

**When to use:** Beginners who need to build a base in each quality before combining them. Also useful for simple peaking (powerlifting meet prep with a known date).

**Trade-off:** You detrain qualities you're not practicing. By the time you reach the power phase, some hypertrophy gains have eroded. Works for beginners because everything grows fast regardless.

**Block lengths:** 3-6 weeks per phase. Total cycle: 12-16 weeks.

### Undulating Periodization (Daily or Weekly)

Vary the training stimulus within each week. Monday heavy (strength), Wednesday moderate (power), Friday light (hypertrophy). Or alternate heavy weeks and light weeks.

**When to use:** Intermediate athletes who respond better to frequent stimulus variation. Good for general strength and hypertrophy goals without a specific peaking date.

**Trade-off:** Hard to peak for a specific event because there's no distinct taper. Better for continuous improvement.

**Structure:** 3-4 different stimulus types rotated through the week or across weeks.

### Conjugate Sequence (Concurrent)

All qualities trained simultaneously with shifting emphasis per phase. Every block includes some strength, some power, and some reactive work — the ratio changes.

**When to use:** Intermediate-to-advanced athletes with a specific performance goal and timeline. The default model for vertical jump, athletic performance, and sport-specific peaking.

**Trade-off:** More complex to program. Requires careful volume management because you're accumulating fatigue across multiple qualities simultaneously.

**Structure:**
- Block 1: 60% strength / 25% power / 15% reactive
- Block 2: 30% strength / 40% power / 30% reactive  
- Block 3: 15% strength / 25% power / 60% reactive

### Block Periodization

Concentrated loading blocks that emphasize one quality heavily (70%+) for 2-4 weeks, with minimal maintenance of other qualities. Then switch.

**When to use:** Advanced athletes who need a very strong stimulus to continue adapting. Also useful when training time is limited and you can't fit everything in every week.

**Trade-off:** Significant detraining of non-emphasized qualities during each block. Requires careful sequencing so blocks build on each other.

**Block lengths:** 2-4 weeks. Short and intense.

## Choosing a Model

| Athlete Tier | Primary Goal | Timeline | Recommended Model |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beginner | General strength | Open-ended | Linear |
| Beginner | Sport performance | 12+ weeks | Linear → Undulating |
| Intermediate | Strength + size | Open-ended | Undulating |
| Intermediate | Peak performance | 12-16 weeks | Conjugate Sequence |
| Advanced | Peak performance | 8-12 weeks | Block or Conjugate |
| Advanced | Competition prep | Known date | Block → Taper |

## Key Concepts Across All Models

### Deload Weeks

Every 3-5 weeks of loading should be followed by a deload: reduce volume by 40-60% while maintaining intensity at 75-80%. This allows connective tissue recovery, CNS restoration, and consolidation of adaptations.

**Rule of 60% (THP):** During deload, total volume drops to roughly 60% of peak week. Intensity stays high enough to maintain neural adaptations.

### Delayed Transformation

Hard training creates both fitness and fatigue simultaneously. The fatigue masks the fitness gains. When you reduce training load (deload or taper), fatigue dissipates faster than fitness — and performance peaks. This is the mechanism behind Block 3 tapers in conjugate models.

### Supercompensation

After a training stimulus and adequate recovery, the body adapts to a level slightly above baseline. The next stimulus should be applied at or near the peak of supercompensation. Too soon → overtraining. Too late → back to baseline.

### Specificity Gradient

As a peaking date approaches, training should become more specific to the target activity. Early blocks can include general preparation. Later blocks should closely mimic the competition movement. For vertical jump: early = squats and general plyos, late = approach jumps and depth jumps.
