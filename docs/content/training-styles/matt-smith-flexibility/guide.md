# Matt Smith Flexibility — Training Style Guide

> **Purpose of this guide.** This document is intended to help coaches — human or AI — design programs using Matt Smith's flexibility methodology. It contains the operational logic, assessments, session templates, decision rules, and common failure modes needed to plan and run a flexibility block for any athlete. Primary sources: (1) **"The Blueprint to Get Flexible Once, Forever"** by Matt Smith (YouTube, Nov 2025, 39 min) — the whole-methodology overview; (2) **MFTK *Side Split Toolkit*** — Matt's purchased course, the productized side-split-specific version of the methodology (curriculum, drill library, phase model, scheduling rules captured in [`sources/mftk-side-split-toolkit-curriculum.md`](sources/mftk-side-split-toolkit-curriculum.md)). The course **confirms** the video's principles (notably the §13 scheduling rule) and **fills in** side-split specifics the video lacked (named drills → §5, phase model → §6, assessment → §3). The course is mostly video/embedded-tool content, so per-drill loading numbers remain **derived from method principles**, not lifted verbatim — those spots stay marked `> NOTE: inferred` or `> TODO` until the videos are transcribed. Other sections still resting on the single video are likewise marked.

A skill-driven flexibility methodology that builds **lasting** mobility by identifying the lowest limiter on each athlete's flexibility ladder, sequencing 3–5 stretches per session to stretch-stack toward a target skill, and scheduling around CNS fatigue so the gains live in the brain — not the warm-up.

---

## 1. When to use this style

**Goal types this serves:**
- Achieving any of the 8 **flexibility skills**: pike (toe touch), pancake, side split, front split, bridge, shoulder flexion (arms overhead), shoulder extension, deep squat
- Sport-specific mobility that bottlenecks technique (e.g., swimmer's streamlined position, climber's wide bridge, acrobat's split, calisthenics athlete's pancake/start-press compression, basketball player's overhead/dunk position)
- Restoring "lost" basic mobility in adults (touching toes, hanging from a bar, sitting on the floor)
- *Unlocking* strength/skill work that's bottlenecked by lack of range (e.g., one-arm handstand, full bridging, deep front rack)

**Athlete profile fit:**
- Training age: **any** — Matt explicitly cites students from teens through 65 years old achieving splits as adults
- Equipment minimum: floor + wall + a resistance band or two; optional: ankle weights for resisted spectrum work; optional: yoga blocks / parallettes
- Time required: **1–2 sessions/week per skill**, ~20–40 min each (3–5 targeted stretches × isometric-holds-for-reps or PIR sets)
  > NOTE: 20–40 min is inferred; the video states "3–5 stretches" and the methods used but doesn't give an explicit session-duration target.
- **Dose-finding (course-sourced):** *start low and build up.* Begin at **1 session per 5 days, or 1–2×/week**; find the **minimum effective dose** first, then climb toward the **optimal effective dose**. When at 2×/week, **spread the sessions** (don't stack them on adjacent days). Two skills → start at 1 session each per week. (Source: Side Split Toolkit, "When and How Often to Train.")
- Compatible co-styles: **strength training, sport practice, vertical-jump, Olympic lifting** — *with* the scheduling constraints in §13. Flexibility creates fatigue and must be sequenced around CNS-demanding work, not stacked the day after.

**Contraindications (do NOT recruit this style if):**
- Athlete is in an acute injury / post-op window where loaded end-range work is medically restricted
  > NOTE: inferred — the video doesn't enumerate medical contraindications, but the methodology relies on aggressive end-range loading (active lifts, isometric holds, eccentric overload) which is inappropriate without clearance.
- Goal is short-term "feeling looser" before an event — the methodology optimizes for **long-term brain adaptations**, not the warm-up viscoelastic gains that last hours. If the athlete needs same-day flexibility, this is the wrong tool (see §2 — Three adaptation categories).
- Athlete cannot tolerate ≥48h of training schedule flexibility around heavy strength/sport days — the scheduling rule in §13 is load-bearing; without it the brain adaptations don't accumulate.

## 2. Mechanism — how this style produces results

**Lead:** Flexibility gains are dominated by **brain (suprapinal) adaptations to stretch tolerance** — your nervous system learning to permit deeper range without protective contraction. Body changes (sarcomerogenesis, tendon compliance) contribute but reverse within ~6 weeks of disuse. Warm-up viscoelastic changes only last hours. The whole methodology — skill focus, stretch stacking, recovery-aware scheduling — is engineered to push brain adaptations specifically and let body adaptations come along for the ride.

### 2.1 The flexibility ladder

Every limiter on an athlete's range is a rung on a ladder: specific muscles, nerve tensions, joint capsules, technique errors, strength deficits at end range, and even fatigue from other training. The athlete's *current* flexibility = the lowest rung. To reach a target skill, *every* rung must be raised to at least that level — but the rate-limiting step is always the lowest one.

**Operational implication:** stretching the popular target (hamstrings for toe-touch) is wrong if the actual limiter for *this* athlete is sciatic nerve tension or weak hip flexors that can't pull them into position. Assessment finds the lowest rung; the program targets it.

### 2.2 Three adaptation categories (and which one matters)

| Category | Mechanism | Time to make | Time to lose | Programming significance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Warm-up (viscoelastic)** | Tissue temperature ↑, fluid redistribution; transient compliance increase | Minutes | Hours | **The trap most people are stuck in.** They stretch for years, feel looser in the session, lose it all by the next day. Not what this style optimizes for. |
| **Body (sarcomerogenesis + tendon)** | Added sarcomeres in series at fiber ends, tendon stiffness shifts | Weeks | **~6 weeks of disuse** | Real adaptation, but reversible. Worth getting, can't be the only target. |
| **Brain (supraspinal stretch tolerance)** | Nervous system raises ceiling on tolerated stretch signal | Weeks–months | **Long-lasting; years in Matt's own case** | The thing this methodology is built to produce. Why students retain range without stretching. |

**Why brain adaptations need protected programming:** the brain decides "how far is safe" based on total *afferent signal load* — including fatigue signals from prior sport/strength training. If your CNS is already saturated with fatigue afferents, the available bandwidth for the brain to raise its stretch ceiling shrinks. Fatigued sessions can produce warm-up gains but not brain adaptations.

### 2.3 The fatigue mechanism — flexibility is not active recovery

Both strength and flexibility training cause CNS fatigue through *the same end pathway*, with different upstream triggers:

```
STRENGTH:     contraction → Ca²⁺ release into sarcomere
FLEXIBILITY:  stretch → stretch-gated channels → Ca²⁺ into sarcomere
                        ↓
              Ca²⁺ accumulates inside the cell
                        ↓
              activates calpains (protein-eating enzymes)
                        ↓
              muscle fiber damage + E-C coupling failure
                        ↓
              local inflammation → bloodstream
                        ↓
              brain detects → registers fatigue
```

This pathway is **delayed by hours** — same as DOMS. It is the operational reason flexibility cannot be programmed as "active recovery" the day after heavy strength (a common and seductive mistake — see §12). The fatigue is real, the brain perceives it, and stretch tolerance adaptations stall.

### 2.4 Stretch stacking — the 1+1=3 effect

When 3–5 stretches target the **same skill**, each one opens range that the next one can use. Stretch A reduces pec restriction → stretch B (lats) can now reach a deeper position → stretch C (thoracic spine) can now extend into range pec+lat were previously gating. The session multiplies instead of summing.

The corollary: doing one stretch each for shoulders, hips, and back is two units of effort that produce two units of gain (no stacking — they don't share a skill). Doing three stretches for shoulder flexion is three units of effort that produce ~five units of gain.

**This is also why session order is opposite of strength training** (see §4): in strength you go compound→isolation (CNS first); in flexibility you go isolation→compound, because each isolation opens range the compound skill needs.

### 2.5 Assisted ↔ resisted spectrum

Every flexibility exercise lives somewhere on this spectrum:

```
heavily assisted ── assisted ── neutral ── resisted ── heavily resisted
       │                                                       │
   bodyweight                                            +load against
   pulling you                                          gravity, into the
   into range                                             same range
```

- **Assisted** training stretches deeper, pushes the ceiling of total range
- **Resisted** training builds *usable* strength inside the range you already have
- **Neutral / middle** develops a little of both

Programming uses **both ends and alternates them within a session** (A1 assisted → A2 resisted). The resisted exercise contracts the antagonist of the muscle just stretched, producing reciprocal inhibition that *deepens* the next assisted attempt. This is a second 1+1=3 effect that stacks on top of skill-aligned stretch stacking.

**Why "more flexible" isn't the right goal for everyone:** Matt cites Sunniva (passively very flexible, low strength-in-range) vs Sundar (less flexible, but strong in the range he has). They need *opposite* programs — Sunniva needs resisted work, Sundar needs assisted work. Assessment determines which.

## 3. Athlete assessment

> Common slot: `concepts/assessment.md`.

The assessment goal is to **locate the lowest rung on the athlete's flexibility ladder for the target skill**. Run this at the start of every block and re-run when progress stalls.

### Baseline tests (run per skill being trained)

| Test category | What it tells you | Example protocol from video |
|---|---|---|
| **Muscle-isolation tests** | Which specific muscle is limiting (vs. which is just "where you feel it") | Shoulder flexion: use bands to load pec major (red), subscap+teres major (green), lats (blue). Position the arm to isolate one at a time — arm back = pec; externally rotated = subscap/teres; rounded low back = lats. Compare which is most restrictive. |
| **Passive vs isometric vs active strength comparison** | Whether the athlete needs more *range* (push the ceiling) or more *strength-in-range* (use what they have) | Compare passive max range against the same range held isometrically against the same range produced actively (e.g., active leg lift). Big gap passive→active → resisted training. Small passive range → assisted training. |
| **Skill-specific simplified test** | Lower-demand version of the target skill | Side split → **Taylor's pose** (butterfly): sit at wall, feet together, knees apart without using hands. Can the calves touch the floor? If no, bent-leg adductor/hip-rotator work is needed before deep side-split work makes sense. |
| **Technique / alignment test** | Whether bone-on-bone or wrong position is the limiter, not tissue | Side split → **pancake hip-tilt test**: open legs, come in slightly, can the head reach the floor with a *rounded* back? If no, hip-tilt range is the side-split limiter. |
| **Nerve-tension test** | Whether nerve mobility is gating range (sciatic for toe-touch, etc.) | Standard slump test / straight-leg-raise with dorsiflexion add. If symptoms reproduce or range collapses with nerve bias, nerve flossing is on the menu. |

> NOTE: The video gives precise protocols only for Taylor's pose and the pancake hip-tilt. For the other six skills, Matt names the test *categories* but doesn't enumerate a battery. Until additional sources are added, the assessor will need to construct skill-specific versions of the four test categories above, using the worked side-split example as the template.

### Classification — what the test results select

| Pattern | Athlete type | Primary programming bias |
|---|---|---|
| Restricted in 1–2 muscles, others fine | Targeted-muscle limiter | Skill-aligned stretches isolating those muscles, stacked toward the compound skill |
| Large passive range, small active range | "Flexible but weak in range" (Sunniva archetype) | Resisted spectrum — isometric holds for reps, active lifts ± eccentric overload |
| Small passive range, small active range | "Tight" (most beginners) | Assisted spectrum — PIR, isometric holds for reps with assist, gradual ROM expansion |
| Restricted nerve tension reproduces symptoms | Nerve-limited | Add flosses/glides/tensioners; reduce passive-stretch volume until nerve mobility improves |
| Range collapses at end position (bone jam / wrong alignment) | Technique-limited | Technique drills + cueing first; this can break years-long plateaus by itself |

### In-block monitoring (capture each session)

- Maximum range achieved on the target skill measurement (e.g., depth in side-split, fingertip-to-floor in pike, bridge height/wrist-to-heel distance)
- Whether the range was held passively, isometrically, or actively
- Subjective stretch tolerance (RPE-style) — is the same depth feeling easier across weeks?
- Did the athlete come in fatigued from prior sport/strength training? (If yes, expect the session to maintain rather than progress.)

## 4. Session structure

> Common slot: `concepts/session-design.md`.

**Ordering rule (and why):** start with **smaller, isolated stretches** and build toward the **compound skill**. This is the **opposite** of strength training (where compound goes first, while CNS is fresh). The reason: stretch stacking — each isolation opens range that the compound skill needs, so the compound goes deeper *because* it came last.

**Session template (one skill, 3–5 stretches, alternating spectrum):**

1. **Isolation / preparatory stretch 1** — assisted-spectrum bias; opens the most restrictive single muscle identified in assessment. Method: usually PIR (contract-relax) or isometric-holds-for-reps. ~3–5 sets.
2. **Isolation / preparatory stretch 2** — resisted-spectrum bias; targets the antagonist or a strength-in-range gap. Method: active lifts ± eccentric overload, or isometric holds. ~3–5 sets.
3. **Isolation / preparatory stretch 3** — assisted again; uses the range freed by stretches 1+2 to address a secondary limiter (often a nerve or joint capsule contribution). Method varies (PIR, joint capsule release, nerve glide).
4. **Compound skill stretch** — the target skill itself, performed with proper technique (see §8). Method: usually isometric holds for reps at the new depth opened by stretches 1–3.
5. **(Optional) Technique drill** — if the assessment flagged technique as a limiter, end with a low-intensity drill that reinforces the cue (e.g., hip-tilt rehearsal for side split).

**Rest intervals:**
- Between sets within a stretch: short (5–15 s) — the rep is the rest
- Between exercises in the same session: ~30–60 s (transition + breath, not full recovery)

> NOTE: rest intervals inferred from Matt's discussion of "3-second holds for reps" with implied short rests; the video does not give an explicit prescription.

**Typical duration:** 20–40 min for a single-skill session. Two-skill sessions (or athletes running two simultaneous skills) cap closer to 50 min.

> NOTE: duration inferred.

**Frequency:** **1–2 sessions/week per skill**. The video repeatedly states this. More is not better — additional volume eats into CNS recovery and crowds out brain adaptations.

## 5. Exercise selection

> Common slot: master library at [`training-styles/exercises.md`](../exercises.md). **The master library currently contains strength exercises only; a flexibility section needs to be added** (Phase-5 TODO).

This style organizes exercises by **method category** rather than by named exercise, because Matt's methodology is method-driven — the same stretch position can be loaded as PIR, isometric-holds-for-reps, or active-lift-with-eccentric depending on what the assessment surfaced.

### The 10 method categories (primary palette)

| # | Method | Spectrum position | When to choose |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **PIR (post-isometric relaxation / contract-relax)** | Assisted | Most common; reps of brief contraction → deeper relaxation. Variant: antagonist-contract-relax. Use when athlete needs to push the passive ceiling. |
| 2 | **Isometric stretching** | Middle / resisted | Hold at end range; some skills *are* isometrics (bridge). Build strength-in-range. |
| 3 | **Isometric holds for reps** *(Matt's most-programmed method)* | Middle / resisted | Instead of one 30-s hold, do 3–5 s holds × multiple reps with short rests. Each rep goes deeper than the previous; total quality is higher than a single long hold. |
| 4 | **Active lifts (± eccentric overload)** | Resisted | Contract the antagonist to pull into position. Eccentric overload variant: lift bent-knee, lower straight-knee — builds strength in a range you can't normally reach actively. |
| 5 | **Dynamic stretching** | Resisted, velocity-loaded | Stretches at speed to engage velocity-sensitive muscle spindles. Use when the goal flexibility needs to transfer to kicks, sprints, throws. |
| 6 | **Ballistic / Rapid Contract-Relax (RCR)** | Assisted, velocity-loaded | Micro-stretch pulses deeper than passive hold allows. Matt prefers **RCR** (self-generated pull) over classical ballistic (momentum) because RCR keeps the athlete in control of depth. |
| 7 | **Joint capsule releases** | Position-dependent | When other methods plateau and the athlete feels "stuck" despite muscle relaxation — capsular restriction is the limiter. Specific positioning to load capsule rather than muscle. |
| 8 | **Nerve flosses / glides / tensioners** | n/a (nerve, not muscle) | When nerve-tension tests reproduce symptoms or cap range. Sciatic nerve only stretches ~8–10% — its mobility is sliding through its sheath, not lengthening. |
| 9 | **Soft tissue work** | n/a | Through-the-stretch positioning of ball/roller, not random foam-rolling. Use when assessment localizes chronic tightness, scar tissue, or trigger points to a specific area. |
| 10 | **Technique drills** | n/a | Not stretching — teaching the alignment that lets the existing range be expressed. Often the single highest-leverage intervention. |

### Substitution map

| If unavailable / contraindicated | Substitute | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Band-based muscle isolation | Manual partner positioning | Same biomechanical isolation, no equipment |
| Ankle weights for resisted lifts | Cable column or partner resistance | Maintains progressive overload at end range |
| Floor-based bridge work | Wall walk / elevated-hand bridge | Reduces shoulder flexion demand while training spine/hip extension |

### Disallowed in this style (with reason)

- **Generic full-body routines** — explicitly rejected. They don't stretch-stack and don't target the athlete's actual ladder limiter.
- **Day-after-strength stretching as "active recovery"** — the fatigue pathway in §2.3 means this isn't recovery; it crowds out brain adaptations.
- **Holding one 30-second stretch** when the goal is end-range strength — use isometric-holds-for-reps instead (deeper per rep, better quality).
- **Forcing range without technique check** — if the athlete is hitting a bone jam (e.g., side split with no hip tilt), adding volume causes pain without progress.

## 6. Periodization

> Common slot: `concepts/periodization.md`.

**Model used:** **skill-focused block** — not a classical linear / undulating / conjugate model. The unit of progression is the *skill*, not the load curve.

**Why this model fits this style:** brain stretch-tolerance adaptations need consistent exposure over weeks; switching skills weekly would prevent any one skill from reaching the threshold where adaptations consolidate. Working one skill (or two related skills) until it hits the target level concentrates the bandwidth.

### Block structure

| Phase | Duration | Emphasis | What the athlete does |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I. Assess** | 1 session | Locate the lowest rung on the ladder for the target skill | Run all five test categories (§3); pick the 3–5 stretches that target the lowest rungs |
| **II. Build** | 6+ weeks (minimum) | Brain adaptation accumulation | Run the session 1–2×/wk; re-test every 2–3 weeks; adjust the 3–5 stretches if a new lower rung appears |
| **III. Realize** | Variable | Express the new range in the target skill / sport context | Pull range into sport-specific movement; less volume, more skill expression |
| **IV. Maintain** | Indefinite | Hold what was built | Most skills self-maintain through overlap (pike maintains front-split front leg). Only re-enter a Build phase if the range erodes. |

> NOTE: Phase III/IV separation inferred from Matt's statement that he and his students "no longer need to stretch" once they've reached their target levels. He does not name explicit phases.

**Course-sourced side-split phase model.** The Side Split Toolkit operationalizes the generic Assess/Build/Realize/Maintain above into named, tool-backed stages specific to the side split:

| Course stage | Maps to generic phase | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| **Assessment** (Side Split Assessment + measuring angles) | I. Assess | Routes the athlete into the correct starting phase by angle + screen results |
| **Phase 1 Program** (Phase 1 Program Builder) | II. Build (beginner) | First structured side-split block for newcomers |
| **Phase 2 & Beyond Program** (Phase 2 Builder [BETA]) | II. Build (intermediate+) | Continued build once Phase 1 is outgrown |
| **Side Split Overload** (Specialist Program) | late Build / Realize | Advanced loaded specialization for athletes pushing the last range |

Two named progression levers the course adds (use *within* a Build phase, not just "add depth"): **Go Deeper, Not Heavier** (bias range over external load — default for already-strong athletes) and **Progressive Underload** (deliberately *reduce* load/assist to keep reps clean on fatigued weeks or skill-exposure days). See `sources/mftk-side-split-toolkit-curriculum.md` §4.

**Frequency is recovery-gated (Phase 1 builder, fully captured).** Start **once every 5 days**; if you recover well and improve each session, progress to **every 4 days → every 3 days**. The rule is *train when the body is recovered* — by recovery quality: **poor → 6–7 days, average → 3–5 days, superior → 2–3 days**. For an athlete also doing heavy strength/jump work (high systemic load = effectively "average" recovery for flex purposes), this lands at **~2×/week max**, which is why the §13 scheduling rule matters. **Stay in Phase 1 until progress stalls (most people 4–6 weeks), then build Phase 2** off the Drill Matrix.

> The Phase 1 Program Builder (drill-selection flowchart + per-drill reps/sets/tempo/rest + example program) is now **fully transcribed** in `sources/mftk-side-split-toolkit-curriculum.md` §6, and the selection logic is summarized in §5 below. The Phase 2 builder and Overload specialist program remain video/embedded-tool-gated.

**Why ≥6 weeks for Build:** body-side adaptations (sarcomerogenesis) reverse within ~6 weeks of disuse. To make the gains stick, the brain side has to be doing the heavy lifting — which requires enough consistent exposure for the supraspinal threshold to shift. The 6-week mark is the floor, not the target.

**Within-block progression rules:**
- **Add depth** when the athlete hits target reps × hold time at current depth across two consecutive sessions
- **Add reps or sets** before adding depth if technique degrades when going deeper
- **Hold** when the athlete is fatigued from sport/strength training that week — maintenance is the win
- **Back off / re-assess** when progress stalls for 2+ weeks at the same depth; the ladder has likely shifted and a new lowest rung appeared

**Between-block progression rules:**
- When the target skill is reached, **switch to a new skill** that doesn't share dominant tissue with the maintained skill (e.g., done with side split → start bridge or shoulder flexion)
- Use **skill overlap** to maintain old skills passively (pike maintains front-split front leg; bridge maintains shoulder extension and hip extension)
- Don't run more than **2 active skills at a time** — bandwidth and recovery cap

## 7. Sample programs

### Worked example — Dennis (acrobat), side split (verbatim from video)

> Context: Dennis is a professional acrobat. Side split is the target skill. Assessment found: can't complete Taylor's pose (short adductors / hip rotators limiting); can't get head to floor in pancake with rounded back (hip-tilt range limiting); and technique-side bone-jam risk in standard side-split position. Three rungs on the ladder, addressed in stack order.

```
SESSION (single-skill, side split):

A1. Bent-leg adductor opener (Taylor's-pose-family position)
    → method: PIR or isometric-holds-for-reps
    → role: open short adductors + hip rotators so the pancake can express deeper range

A2. Pancake (legs wide, hinge forward)
    → method: isometric-holds-for-reps (3-s holds × multiple reps)
    → role: extend hip-tilt range, which the side-split position needs

A3. Side-split position w/ hip-tilt cue OR external-rotation cue (feet up)
    → method: isometric-holds-for-reps at new depth opened by A1+A2
    → role: target skill; the depth here is unlocked by everything above

A4. Technique drill — hip-tilt rehearsal in shallow side-split position
    → role: reinforce the alignment cue that prevents the bone jam

Frequency: 1–2× per week. Stretch-stack: each exercise unlocks the next.
```

### Generic skill-block template (any skill)

> NOTE: This template is inferred from the Dennis example + Matt's general principles. The video does not give a multi-week loading scheme.

```
WEEK 1 (assessment + intro):
  Session 1: Full skill-specific assessment (5 test categories). Pick 3-5 stretches.
  Session 2: Run the session at conservative depth. Record baseline depth on each stretch.

WEEKS 2-3 (build, low depth):
  Session 1+2: Run the same 3-5 stretches. Add 1 rep/set per week. Hold depth or add modestly.

WEEKS 4-6 (build, push depth):
  Session 1+2: Add depth when reps × hold time complete cleanly. Reassess at week 6.

WEEK 6 (reassess):
  Re-run test categories. If a new lowest rung appeared, swap one of the 3-5 stretches for it.

WEEKS 7-12 (continue or transition):
  Continue if skill not yet at target. If target reached, transition to Realize → Maintain.
  Begin next skill at week 12 if maintaining one is enough.
```

## 8. Technique

Technique is **load-bearing** in this methodology. Matt's worked examples include cases where a technique change alone (no new exercises, no more volume) broke through plateaus that had lasted years. Skip this section at the athlete's peril.

### Side split — hip tilt vs hip jam

- **Cue:** Tilt the hips **anteriorly** (forward) before sliding the legs apart. Alternatively, **externally rotate** the legs (point feet up) — same hip-alignment outcome. Both cues prevent femoral-head-on-acetabulum impingement.
- **Common error:** Opening legs straight to the side with neutral or posterior pelvic tilt. The femur jams against the hip socket bone-on-bone; further range is biomechanically impossible and forcing it produces hip pain.
- **Drill:** Practice the hip-tilt motion in a *shallow* side-split width (legs maybe 60–90°) before going deeper. The alignment must be automatic before depth is added.

### Bridge — lift up, then arch over

- **Cue:** From the supine setup, **press up first** (full elbow + knee extension), *then* shift weight forward to drop the chest over the wrists. Don't try to arch immediately.
- **Common error:** Trying to arch the spine before the shoulders are fully open / arms locked — produces a "low bridge" with all the load on lumbar spine and none on thoracic extension or shoulder flexion.
- **Drill:** Wall walks (back to wall, hands climb down) to build the lift-up-then-arch-over sequence in a supported position.

### Front split — hips square

- **Cue:** Keep both **hip points facing forward** as the legs separate. The back hip will want to externally rotate to relieve the stretch on the hip flexor — resist this.
- **Common error:** Letting the back hip open (external rotation) makes the position "feel" deeper but converts the front split into a half-pancake. Long-term gain stalls because the actual front-split tissues aren't loaded.
- **Drill:** Tall-kneeling split position with the back foot tucked under, hips actively rotated to face forward. Lower into split depth only when squareness can be maintained.

### Skill-specific notes flagged by the video

- Pancake: hinge from the hips, *rounded* back is acceptable (and necessary for the hip-tilt-test version)
- Pike: same as pancake — hip hinge first, spine roundness is permitted at depth
- Squat: technique-driven but specific cueing not detailed in source → TODO

## 9. Recovery & nutrition

> Common slot: `concepts/recovery.md` for universal floors (sleep, protein, hydration).

**Style-specific recovery demands:**
- Flexibility training **creates CNS fatigue** via the calcium → calpain → inflammation pathway (see §2.3). It is **not active recovery**.
- Allow **≥24h** between a heavy flexibility session and the next sport/strength session that would compete for CNS bandwidth.
- The reverse — putting flexibility on a fatigued day — is the dominant failure mode (see §12 and §13).

**Nutritional priorities for this style:**
- No style-specific nutrition guidance is given in the source. Default to the universal protein floor (~0.8–1 g/lb bodyweight) and adequate hydration; the inflammation produced by flexibility training is the same kind that responds to standard recovery nutrition.

> NOTE: Source does not address nutrition. This section will stay thin until additional sources are added.

**In-season modifications:**
- During heavy in-season competition, **drop to maintenance frequency** (1×/week or even bi-weekly per skill). The 6-week reversal threshold gives a ~6-week grace period before maintenance falls off — long enough that mid-season skill drift is rare if the build phase reached target.

## 10. Coach's notes

**Tacit knowledge from the video that doesn't fit other slots:**

- **Matt's own progress timeline:** 6 years of "normal" stretching produced almost no flexibility. He was "genetically not flexible." The ladder approach was what worked after that — proof point that the methodology is built for non-natural-flexies, not maintenance for already-flexible people.

- **Isometric-holds-for-reps is his most-programmed method.** Multiple short holds (3 s) outperform single long holds (30 s) because the short rest between reps lets the next rep go deeper than the previous. Watch for athletes who default to long holds out of habit and switch them.

- **RCR > classical ballistic stretching.** Same end (deeper micro-pulses than passive allows), but RCR keeps the athlete in control of depth. He learned classical ballistic from Shaolin monks and moved away from it. Default to RCR when the methodology calls for ballistic-style work.

- **Brain adaptations persist for years.** Matt himself can drop into a side split cold without warm-up despite not having "trained" the side split in 3+ years. Don't underestimate maintenance via overlap — once a skill is built right, it stays.

- **Technique fixes can break years-long plateaus.** Matt's student Theo had a "mental barrier" on a position; a small cueing change (not a new exercise, not more volume) was the entire fix. Always check technique before adding load or volume.

- **Skill overlap is real and exploitable:**
  - Pike maintains front-split (front leg) and pancake (hamstring chain)
  - Bridge maintains shoulder extension + hip extension + spine extension
  - Pancake maintains side-split adductor work
  - Use this when planning multi-skill arcs — pick non-overlapping skills for max efficiency, or overlapping skills if you want one session to do double duty.

- **Don't program too many skills at once.** "Most people try to do too much. It's better to make progress where you need it now... than try to be overly ambitious and just end up quitting." Two skills is the cap; one is often better.

- **Adult learners can build it.** Don't assume the window closes — Matt's students range up to 65 years old hitting splits as adults. Programming changes (gentler ramps, more recovery between sessions), but the methodology is the same.

- **The "feeling looser" trap.** Athletes who stretch and "feel great" right after but lose it all by the next day are stuck in warm-up viscoelastic gains. They're not adapting; they're just warming up over and over. If sessions feel productive *in* the session but progress isn't accumulating week-over-week, this is the diagnosis.

## 11. Programming decision tree

> Common slot: `concepts/decision-framework.md`.

### Step 1 — Pick the target skill(s)

- **Inputs:** athlete's sport, daily-life needs, expressed mobility complaint, and any technique bottleneck identified during sport coaching
- **Decision:** select **1–2 of the 8 flexibility skills** (pike, pancake, side split, front split, bridge, shoulder flexion, shoulder extension, squat)
- **Bias toward overlap if possible** — if two skills are wanted, pick a pair that share tissue (e.g., front split + pike both hit hamstrings) so the same sessions do double duty

### Step 2 — Assess to find the lowest rung on the ladder

- **Inputs:** the target skill, the athlete's body
- **Run all five test categories from §3:**
  1. Muscle-isolation (which specific muscle is most restrictive?)
  2. Passive vs isometric vs active comparison (range gap or strength gap?)
  3. Simplified skill test (e.g., Taylor's pose for side split)
  4. Technique / alignment test (bone jam? wrong cue?)
  5. Nerve-tension test (is a nerve the gate?)
- **Output:** ranked list of 2–4 limiters, top of list = lowest rung

### Step 3 — Select 3–5 stretches that stack toward the skill

- **Rule:** each stretch must target a *specific* rung from Step 2's list
- **Sequence:** isolation → isolation → isolation → compound skill (optionally + technique drill)
- **Alternate spectrum:** A1 assisted → A2 resisted → A3 assisted → A4 compound (or compound-with-isometric-holds)
- **Pick the method per stretch** from the §5 palette of 10:
  - Passive ceiling needs raising → PIR or RCR (assisted)
  - Strength in range needs raising → isometric holds for reps or active lifts (resisted)
  - Nerve limiter → flosses/glides/tensioners
  - Joint capsule limiter → joint capsule release
  - Chronic tightness localized → through-the-stretch soft tissue work
  - Technique limiter → drill that rehearses the cue

### Step 4 — Set volume, intensity, and schedule

| Component | Building volume | Maintenance volume |
|---|---|---|
| Sessions per skill per week | 2 | 1 (or 1×/2 weeks once target reached) |
| Stretches per session | 3–5 | 2–3 (focus on compound + one isolation) |
| Sets per stretch | 3–5 | 2–3 |
| Reps per set (isometric-holds-for-reps) | 5–10 reps × 3-s holds | 3–5 reps × 3-s holds |
| Depth | Push by 1 increment when target reps clean | Hold current depth |
| Total session time | 20–40 min | 10–20 min |

> NOTE: volume table inferred from Matt's qualitative prescriptions (e.g., "3–5 stretches, 3-s holds for reps, 1–2× per week"). Exact set/rep numbers are derived; the principle is sourced.

### Step 5 — Place the session in the weekly schedule

> See §13 for the full rule. Quick rule of thumb:
- **First choice:** 2+ days after the most CNS-demanding sport/strength session of the week
- **Second choice:** Same day as a sport/strength session, separated AM/PM (sport AM, flexibility PM)
- **Third choice:** Immediately after a strength session, same training block, with reduced volume on both
- **Avoid:** day after heavy strength; do not treat as "active recovery"

### Step 6 — Re-assess every 2–3 weeks

- Re-run the test categories
- If a new lower rung has appeared (e.g., adductors were limiting, now nerve tension is the new limiter), **swap one of the 3–5 stretches**
- If progress is stalled at the same depth for 2+ weeks: technique check first, then re-test the spectrum (maybe an assisted limiter has become a strength-in-range limiter)
- If goal level reached: **transition to maintenance** and start the next skill

## 12. Common mistakes

1. **Stretching where you feel it.** Choosing stretches by sensation, not by assessment. The muscle you feel the stretch in is often *not* the actual limiter — your body routes the stretch to whatever is most compliant, which is usually the muscle you've been stretching most. Fix: assessment-driven stretch selection from §3.

2. **Session order copied from strength training.** Doing the compound skill stretch first, then "accessory" isolations. This wastes the stretch-stacking effect — the compound has no opened range to express. Fix: isolation → compound order from §4.

3. **Stretching the day after heavy strength.** The fatigue pathway in §2.3 means the brain is registering inflammation; available stretch-tolerance bandwidth is consumed by fatigue afferents. You'll feel like you're working hard and get no brain adaptation. Fix: §13 scheduling rule.

4. **Calling flexibility "active recovery" and programming it on off-days as a recovery tool.** Same root cause as #3 — flexibility training is its own CNS stressor. Fix: it's training, schedule it like training.

5. **Full-body routines instead of skill-focused sessions.** Spreading 3–5 stretches across shoulder, hip, back, ankle = one unit of gain per stretch, no stacking. Fix: pick one skill per session; all 3–5 stretches align to it.

6. **Holding 30-second isometrics when isometric-holds-for-reps would go deeper.** Single long holds plateau in depth because there's no inter-rep relaxation to permit deeper movement. Fix: 3–5 s holds × 5–10 reps with short rests.

7. **Mistaking warm-up viscoelastic gains for real adaptation.** Stretching, feeling looser, losing it by next day, doing it again. This is a years-long trap that produces no progress. Fix: track week-over-week max depth at a *cold* re-test, not in-session depth.

8. **Side split with no hip tilt → bone jam.** Femur impinges on acetabulum; further range is biomechanically impossible. Forcing it produces hip pain, not flexibility. Fix: technique check (§8). This single fix has broken multi-year plateaus.

9. **Trying to improve everywhere at once.** Three skills simultaneously = no skill gets enough CNS exposure for brain adaptation. Fix: 1–2 skills max; use overlap to passively maintain the rest.

10. **Ignoring nerve tension.** Tight-feeling hamstrings that don't respond to hamstring work may be sciatic-limited. Stretching the hamstring harder produces nerve symptoms, not range. Fix: §3 nerve-tension test; nerve flosses if positive.

11. **Stopping flexibility training and assuming it's all gone.** Body-side adaptations reverse in ~6 weeks, but brain adaptations persist for years. Athletes who stop after hitting their target skill often retain most of the range. Fix: trust the brain-adaptation persistence; transition to maintenance instead of quitting.

12. **Adding load before fixing technique.** When an athlete plateaus, the instinct is more volume / deeper depth / heavier resistance. Often the actual fix is a small cueing change (Theo example). Fix: technique check before any load progression.

## 13. Style interactions

> Common slot: `concepts/style-interactions.md`.

### Day-pairing rules

- **NEVER** put a flexibility session on the day *after* a heavy strength or high-CNS sport session — the fatigue pathway (§2.3) caps stretch-tolerance adaptation that day
- **NEVER** treat flexibility as "active recovery" and schedule it on off days following heavy work
- **PREFER** flexibility on **dedicated training days** that are themselves followed by ≥24h before the next CNS-demanding work

### Three-tier scheduling priority (load-bearing rule — now course-confirmed)

> Originally inferred from the free video; the Side Split Toolkit's "When and How Often to Train" lesson states the **same three configurations explicitly** as the options for combining flexibility with interfering sport/strength (AM/PM split · ≥2 days after · same-session with reduced volume). Treat this rule as confirmed, not inferred.


| Priority | Configuration | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| **1st (best)** | Flexibility session **≥2 days after** the most recent CNS-demanding session | Inflammation cleared, brain bandwidth restored, full adaptation possible |
| **2nd** | **Same-day split**: sport/strength AM, flexibility PM | Inflammation hasn't peaked yet (it's delayed by hours); afternoon stretch tolerance is still close to baseline |
| **3rd** | **Same session**: flexibility immediately after strength, reduced volume on both | Inflammation building but pre-peak; works in tight schedules but caps total volume of either component |
| **Avoid** | Day **after** heavy strength/sport | Peak inflammation; brain registers fatigue; warm-up gains only |

### Recovery overlap with other styles

- **Shares CNS budget with**: heavy strength training, sprinting, high-volume jumps, Olympic lifts
- **Does NOT share CNS budget with**: low-intensity skill practice, technique walkthroughs, isometric posture/breathing work, easy cardio
- **Cap**: in a 6-day training week with 4 high-CNS strength/sport days, 1–2 flexibility sessions fit; more crowds the recovery budget

### Calorie / load conflicts

- Negligible direct calorie cost (low metabolic demand)
- The **adaptation cost** competes with strength/hypertrophy adaptations because both compete for inflammation-resolution capacity. In a heavy hypertrophy or strength block, expect flexibility progress to slow.

### Equipment / facility conflicts

- Minimal — bands, floor space, ankle weights. Compatible with home and gym facilities.
- Soft-tissue work (method #9) sometimes needs a wall + ball + privacy.

### Compatible co-styles

- **Vertical-jump training** — works well in build phases of a jump arc; the flexibility skill (e.g., deep squat, shoulder flexion for arm swing) directly serves the jump
- **Olympic lifting** — front-rack mobility, deep squat, overhead position are all flexibility-skill territory; high alignment
- **General strength** — compatible with the §13 scheduling rule; flexibility complements strength when properly sequenced
- **Sport practice** — every sport has at least one bottleneck skill (swimming → bridge; calisthenics → pancake; basketball → overhead/hip extension)

### Integrating a side-split build into a concurrent jump/strength arc

A reusable pattern for running this style as a **secondary skill (2×/week)** alongside a primary power/strength/sport arc, without taxing the primary goal. Athlete-agnostic; for a worked instantiation see the athlete bundle that references this section.

**1. Identify the protected day.** Find the arc's single most important performance/peak day (jump day, test day, competition). Loaded adductor/end-range work saps force for **24–48 h**, so the rule is: **no loaded or deep side-split work within 48 h of that day.** This usually rules out the 1–2 days before it.

**2. Place two sessions, spread, both clear of the protected day** (Matt's 2×/week, spaced — see §1, §6):
- **Deep loaded "Build" session** — the progress driver. Put it on a low-leg/recovery day that sits **≥48–72 h before** the protected day. Run the full stretch-stack (opener → strength-in-range → compound → technique) with the Phase-1 parameters (§5).
- **Light active/technique session** — placed at the **maximum distance** from the protected day (often the day after it, kept *light* so the day-after-high-CNS rule isn't violated). No external load — pure "go deeper, not heavier" skill exposure + the Kneeling-Tilt technique drill.

**3. Treat high systemic load as reduced recovery.** An athlete doing 4+ high-CNS strength/sport days/week is, for flexibility purposes, in "average" recovery → cap at **~2×/week** (§6 recovery-gated frequency). Don't chase the "superior recovery → every 2–3 days" cadence during a hard concurrent block.

**4. Phase it to protect the primary peak.** Run the flexibility **Build hard during the arc's accumulation blocks**, then drop to **maintenance (1×/week, compound + one opener + technique)** during the primary goal's **peak/competition block**. Brain stretch-tolerance adaptations persist for years and body-side adaptations take ~6 weeks to reverse, so a 4–6 week maintenance window **holds** the range while freeing recovery for the peak (this is the §9 in-season modification applied to a concurrent peak).

**5. Measure in both currencies.** Keep whatever distance metric the arc's goal contract uses, and **add the course's angle measurement** (§3) as the progression trigger — depth-before-load decisions key off angle.

> Why this respects both goals: every loaded touch is ≥48 h from the protected day; the second session is intentionally light and far from it; and the build is front-loaded so the taper/peak block isn't competing for recovery.

### Incompatible co-styles

- **Daily high-CNS training with no rest days** — there's nowhere to put flexibility without violating the day-after rule
- **Pure peaking / tapering blocks for a strength meet** — competing adaptation demands; pause flexibility build phases during a meet peak
