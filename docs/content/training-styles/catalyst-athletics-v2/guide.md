## 1. When to use this style

**Goal types this serves:**
- Competitive Olympic weightlifting (snatch + clean & jerk) [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- Peaking for 2–3 priority meets per year while training through smaller meets [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- Long-term technical development through targeted exercise variation [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]

**Athlete profile fit:**
- Training age: any — programming and percentages are adjusted to individual neurological efficiency and training history [^vid-SInXEdJVTA8][^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Equipment minimum: standard weightlifting gym (barbell, platforms, blocks for some variations) [^vid-dUhU45y5DRk]
- Time required: typically 5 days/week, but can be programmed effectively for 2–6 days [^vid-mzzmZAWxOn4]
- Compatible co-styles: general strength training is integrated (squats, push press, pulls) [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]

**Contraindications:**
- Athletes who can't or won't track training data — this style depends on records to refine future prescriptions [^vid-SInXEdJVTA8][^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Don't schedule a local meet within 2 weeks of a primary competition [^vid-5FscVghWSps]

## 2. Mechanism — how this style produces results

**Lead:** Adaptation comes from progressively dosing intensity, volume, and frequency so the body overcompensates between sessions, then chaining those microcycles into mesocycles and macrocycles that progressively focus on the competition lifts to peak on demand [^vid-_Q0uEiiRWYs].

### Progressive overload requires the right training-to-recovery ratio
Identical demands eventually produce zero adaptation [^vid-_Q0uEiiRWYs]. Train too frequently and you regress because fatigue accumulates faster than adaptation [^vid-_Q0uEiiRWYs]. Train too infrequently and elevated ability fades back to or below baseline [^vid-_Q0uEiiRWYs]. Massive infrequent doses don't work for weightlifting because skill, confidence, and the mental side suffer with long breaks [^vid-_Q0uEiiRWYs].

### Individualization over demographics
The same prescription can be too easy for one athlete and too hard for another, or appropriate for one exercise and not another [^vid-SInXEdJVTA8][^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]. Female athletes generally have lower neurological efficiency and can do more reps at a given percentage than males [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8][^vid-tqs6UpgqQDg]. Newer lifters are similar and may need higher percentages for basic strength work [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]. Always prescribe based on the individual, not the demographic [^vid-tqs6UpgqQDg].

### Fix causes, not symptoms
Lagging lifts should be addressed by exercises that target the actual technical cause, not by simply doing more of the lift — "just adding more jerks to the week isn't going to magically fix it. It's just going to be more practice doing it wrong" [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]. Heels rising before the power position, for example, isn't inherently wrong; what matters is why [^vid-ra8dYrUKJys].

### Variation is real, dogma is dangerous
There is no single correct technical style — "if there were one true way, we would see it in every lifter at the highest levels of competition" [^vid-dUhU45y5DRk]. Not every technical flaw should be fixed: the cost of change must be weighed against potential benefit [^vid-dUhU45y5DRk].

## 3. Athlete assessment

**Lift ratios are the primary diagnostic.** Use typical competition-to-strength lift ratios to balance work. If an athlete has a ton of squat strength already but low snatch and clean & jerk, reduce squat work and push the competition lifts [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]. The same Prilepin-fitting prescription is "a completely different level of demand for an athlete whose clean and jerk is 95% of their best front squat versus 70%" [^vid-XsqLP70vK5Y].

**Effort style on maxes.** Athletes who blow themselves out on every max get percentages reduced; athletes who cruise through max lifts get percentages bumped up [^vid-tqs6UpgqQDg][^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8].

**RPE self-evaluation accuracy.** Have athletes write down what they think the RPE was on top sets even when prescribed by other means, so the coach can check accuracy and the athlete can practice [^vid-tqs6UpgqQDg].

**Track everything.** "It's so important to track your athletes' training data so that you have records to base future program choices on rather than having to just rely on basic guidelines like Prilepin's table" [^vid-SInXEdJVTA8][^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8].

**New-athlete onboarding.** Start with their previous training frequency unless it's clearly wrong, then adjust based on data [^vid-mzzmZAWxOn4]. When determining volume, always start low and work up [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI].

## 4. Session structure

**Ordering rule:** Work from higher to lower technical and speed demand within a session — e.g., snatch → snatch pull → back squat [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]. Two exceptions: (1) technique primers go immediately before the lift they prime, even if out of conventional order [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]; (2) early in a cycle for a lifter needing leg strength, squats can go first so they're done fresh and the comp lifts force focus under fatigue [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY].

**Practical ordering also minimizes equipment changes** — e.g., do snatch pulls right after snatches rather than swapping to jerks in between [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY].

**Group competition-lift-related exercises together** — if snatch is the primary lift in a session, pulls/deadlifts should be snatch variety [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY].

**Morning + afternoon split (training camp / advanced model):** Morning is technique/primer work specific to that day's afternoon main session [^vid-dUhU45y5DRk].

**Anytime snatch or clean pulls are programmed**, warm up with a pull drill: start from power position, follow with the lowest hang position the athlete can currently do well, a few sets of 3–5 total pulls [^vid-Ou5X6bNVuhE].

## 5. Programming principles

### Year plan
- Think of a year as roughly four 12-week macrocycles [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- Only truly peak for 2–3 meets per year at the national/international level; train through the rest [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- Allocate longer macrocycles (e.g., 16 weeks) to the most important meet, shorter (e.g., 12 weeks) to secondary meets [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- Beginner/intermediate lifters can have one macrocycle per competition, averaging 8–9 weeks [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- A 3-month gap for a newer lifter is better split into two 6-week macrocycles than one 12-week [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- The initial post-season macrocycle focuses on capacity, technique fixes, and general strength with more variety and volume — not GPP [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- A transition week is a lighter, easier version of the *next* macrocycle, not the previous one [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- After a major competition, schedule a "ladies' choice" week: 2–3 days of easy, different, fun training [^vid-5FscVghWSps]

### Mesocycle
- A 12-week macrocycle splits into three 4-week mesocycles [^vid-5FscVghWSps]
- As a cycle progresses, reduce exercise variations and shift focus toward the competition lifts [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]
- Early in cycle: halting deadlifts for pulls → progress to pull-to-hip → eventually complete pulls [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]
- Early in cycle: snatch balance progressions go snatch push press + OHS → snatch balance + OHS → snatch balance [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]
- Typical 4-week volume distribution: 30%, 27%, 23%, 20% of total, with intensity rising weeks 1–3 then dropping week 4 [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]
- Volumes can be reordered (e.g., 30%, 23%, 27%, 20%) to break up the largest weeks [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]
- Competition prep mesocycles use more dramatic reductions (e.g., 35%, 28%, 22%, 15%) [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]
- Total comp-prep volume can be 25–35% lower than the corresponding prep phase [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]
- Week-to-week volume change is typically 10–15% [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]

### Week
- 5 days is the most common; effective programs can run 2–6 days [^vid-mzzmZAWxOn4]
- Standard 5-day: Mon/Tue/Wed/Fri/Sat or Mon/Tue/Wed/Thu/Sat [^vid-mzzmZAWxOn4]
- 4-day: Mon/Tue/Thu/Sat; 3-day: Mon/Wed/Fri or Sat [^vid-mzzmZAWxOn4]
- Alternate big and small days; Tuesday is the lowest workload (between two big days) [^vid-mzzmZAWxOn4]
- Friday can be the biggest day, often combining heavy snatch + C&J + squats [^vid-mzzmZAWxOn4]
- Spread exposure to each competition lift evenly across the week for skill practice and balanced stress [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]
- Clean and jerk usually gets slightly less volume than snatch because it's more taxing and less skill-demanding [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]

### Reps & intensity
- Strength work: 1–5 reps at 80–100%, biased toward 1–3 reps [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Speed/power work: 1–3 reps at 60–75% [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Technique work: 1–3 reps at 50–70% [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Hypertrophy: 5+ reps at 60–80%, closer to failure than usual [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Recovery work: 2–3 reps at sub-60% [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Push to high end of rep range for strength lifts (squats); stay at low end for snatch/clean/jerk to preserve technique [^vid-SInXEdJVTA8]
- High-rep squats (sets of 10) are used at most 3–4 weeks per year [^vid-dUhU45y5DRk]

### Intensity prescription
- RPE is preferred over RIR for Olympic weightlifting because low reps make the RIR scale too coarse [^vid-tqs6UpgqQDg][^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Prescribe actual weights when you have enough history with the athlete [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- **Bold rep numbers** signal "you may add reps if feeling good, target ~RPE 8–9, not a max" — used on final set of strength exercises, often before deload [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- On heavy total days, prescribe a number of singles with a minimum weight and bold the top sets [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Mix prescription methods (kg, %, RPE) within a single program based on what fits each exercise/phase [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Prilepin's table is a "laugh test" sanity check, not a precise prescription — and is better suited to strength lifts than the competition lifts [^vid-XsqLP70vK5Y][^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]
- Only count reps at >60% (some coaches use 70%); be consistent per athlete [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]
- Count each distinct exercise in a complex as its own rep [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]
- A clean & jerk single counts as 2 reps (clean + jerk) [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]
- Track accessory volume separately from primary volume [^vid-dIQE4xnFyNI]

### Pulls for newer lifters
Prescribe pulls/deadlifts somewhat independently of snatch/clean rather than as a fixed % of the competition lift, since typical pull loading off a technique-limited comp lift will be too light [^vid-tqs6UpgqQDg][^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8].

## 6. Sample sessions

### Conventional 5-day week, early mesocycle [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]

**Monday** — Snatch variation, snatch pull variation, back squat (higher reps, lower intensity, slow eccentric first rep, pause first 2–3 reps) [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]

**Tuesday** — Jerk variation, snatch technique exercise, push press or press variation with overhead holds + overhead carry after last rep, upper body pushing accessories [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]

**Wednesday** — Clean (maybe with jerk), clean pull, pause front squat (pause all reps, lower intensity, emphasize maximal concentric speed) [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]

**Friday** — Snatch + clean & jerk (heavy total), back squat (highest intensity squat day, lower reps, conventional) [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]

**Saturday** — Snatch and clean technique work (e.g., slow pull snatches, power cleans), snatch-related overhead strength (snatch balance with 3s pause), upper body pulling accessories [^vid-XpN5dGyHKqY]

### Training camp snatch day, early cycle [^vid-dUhU45y5DRk]

**Morning (primer/technique):** Muscle snatch / power position snatch / power snatch — emphasis on turnover, quick finish, quick change of direction [^vid-dUhU45y5DRk]

**Afternoon (main session, squats first early in cycle):**
1. Back squat 10s *or* front squat triples [^vid-dUhU45y5DRk]
2. Snatch — triples at moderate weight (or from blocks if managing back/hip issues) [^vid-dUhU45y5DRk]

### Warm-up before any session with snatch or clean pulls [^vid-Ou5X6bNVuhE]
- Pull from power position
- Pull from lowest hang position currently performed well
- A few sets of 3–5 total pulls each — keep volume low [^vid-Ou5X6bNVuhE]

### Heavy total day [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]
- Snatch: prescribed number of singles, minimum weight given, top sets bolded so the athlete can push if feeling ready
- Clean & jerk: same format [^vid-uQeJIGkJmN8]

### 6-day variant — Thursday addition [^vid-mzzmZAWxOn4]
Add a small Thursday session with light technique work, accessory work, and mobility — kept as the lowest-workload day of the week to preserve Friday [^vid-mzzmZAWxOn4].
