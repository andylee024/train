# Catalyst Athletics — Olympic Weightlifting Style Guide

> **Purpose of this guide.** This document is for coaches — human or AI — designing programs in the Olympic weightlifting style as practiced and taught by Greg Everett and Catalyst Athletics. It is synthesized from the 2016 edition of *Olympic Weightlifting: A Complete Guide for Athletes & Coaches* (1,267 pages). The bar: another coach can pick this up and run a program from it.

---

*An Olympic weightlifting style for athletes pursuing competitive snatch + clean & jerk performance — technique-driven, with periodization built around lifting meet peaks.*

---

## 1. When to use this style

**Goal types this serves:**
- Competitive Olympic weightlifting (snatch + clean & jerk maxima)
- Athletes who need explosive triple-extension power as a transferable trait (athletic-prep contexts where the lift IS the means, not just a goal)
- Restoration of speed-strength and explosive-strength in athletes whose absolute strength has saturated (the "Explosive Strength Deficit" case from §13 in Everett)

**Athlete profile fit:**
- Training age: **any** (with technique-learning phase scaled to biological + training age; ideal start is 10–14, but adult learners are explicitly supported via Skill Level 0 protocols)
- Equipment minimum: barbell + bumper plates + platform + squat rack + pulling blocks (jerk blocks ideal)
- Time required: **3–6 sessions/week**, 60–120 min depending on level; advanced lifters split into multiple daily sessions
- Compatible co-styles: general strength, jump training, mobility/flexibility, GPP (well-defined integrations per §13)

**Contraindications (do NOT recruit this style if):**
- Athlete cannot achieve safe overhead and front-rack positions and is unwilling or unable to invest in mobility work — these lifts are non-negotiable on position
- Active shoulder/wrist/back/knee injury that prevents the catch positions and cannot be substituted around (e.g., the rack position for the clean is structural — no front rack = no full clean)
- Pure absolute-strength or pure hypertrophy goal with no need for power expression — a powerlifting or bodybuilding template is more direct
- Sub-acute soft tissue injuries where high-velocity loading is contraindicated
- No access to bumper plates or a surface where lifts can be safely dropped — the lifts cannot be safely performed without this

## 2. Mechanism — how this style produces results

**Lead:** The snatch and clean & jerk are unique because the competition lifts themselves are the most effective training exercises for the qualities they require — a degree of specificity not commonly possible in other strength sports. Programming therefore arranges progressive overload of these specific lifts (and their close variations) while balancing the three models of adaptation (GAS, fitness-fatigue, supercompensation) against the body's recovery capacity.

### Three models of adaptation (use all three to reason about training)

- **General Adaptation Syndrome:** Alarm → Resistance → Exhaustion. After a training bout, performance drops (Alarm), then super-compensates as the body adapts (Resistance). Exhaustion = overtraining, the state you actively avoid through volume/intensity modulation and recovery.
- **Supercompensation:** an unfamiliar stress, given adequate recovery, produces adaptation *beyond* the original demand. This is the basis of progressive overload.
- **Fitness-Fatigue:** every training bout creates simultaneous fitness gains AND fatigue. Fitness persists longer than fatigue (~3:1 ratio for an average session). The job of programming is to schedule subsequent training so that fitness is preserved while fatigue dissipates enough to train productively again.

These models don't dictate prescription precisely — they create the *vocabulary* the coach uses to explain why volume drops over a competition mesocycle, why a deload is timed where it is, and why subsequent training must occur before newly developed capacity decays.

### Strength qualities (the F-V curve, Catalyst terminology)

Four distinct qualities, each requiring specific training:

| Quality | Definition | Trained by |
|---|---|---|
| **Absolute strength** | Max force regardless of speed | Heavy singles/doubles in squat, pull |
| **Speed-strength** | Max contraction velocity against resistance | Competition lifts at moderate-high % with maximal intent |
| **Explosive-strength** | Max force in minimal time (rate of force development) | Snatch/clean from blocks, jumps, light dynamic work |
| **Strength endurance** | Sustained reps over time | Higher-rep accessory work; not a primary focus |

Critical insight (Zatsiorsky / Medvedyev): **at advanced levels there is no correlation between absolute strength and speed-strength in the same movement.** You cannot drive max snatch by squat alone past a certain point. The Explosive Strength Deficit (ESD) — the gap between absolute strength and force expressed in a fast movement — must be trained directly, not assumed away.

### SAID — specificity of adaptation

The body adapts specifically to the demands imposed. The snatch and clean & jerk are nuanced combinations of strength, speed, explosiveness, precision, timing, focus and confidence; *no other exercise replicates them.* This is why the competition lifts themselves must constitute a significant portion of training volume — proportional to the athlete's level (more for advanced, less for beginners still building base strength).

The corollary: **non-specific physical activity (long-distance running, heavy bodybuilding, sport-irrelevant conditioning) limits adaptation** both by direct physiological competition for the desired qualities and by competing for recovery capacity.

### Genetic potential

A real factor, not a moral judgment. Body proportions (brachiomorphic = short legs/long torso = squat-and-clean-friendly; dolicomorphic = long legs/short torso = harder for weightlifting), muscle fiber composition, hormonal levels, joint structure (especially elbow hyperextension), and motor learning ability all set ceilings. Program design must take the athlete you have, not the athlete you wish you had — exploit strengths, mitigate weaknesses.

## 3. Athlete assessment

> Common slot: `concepts/assessment.md`. Catalyst-specific instantiation below.

**Baseline tests** (run before any program design):

| Test | Protocol | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Chronological + biological age** | Date of birth + maturation status (puberty stage for youth) | Window of optimal trainability; competitive classification (youth/junior/senior/master) |
| **Height : bodyweight ratio** | Standard measurement, compare to Catalyst tables 33.1 (M) / 33.2 (F) | Optimal weight class fit; leverage profile |
| **Body type** | Visual: brachiomorphic / mesomorphic / dolicomorphic | Predicted strength vs. snatch/CJ ratios; mobility risk |
| **Mobility — wrists** | Athlete attempts overhead position (snatch) and rack position (clean); evaluate ROM | Whether snatch/clean catch positions are achievable |
| **Mobility — elbows** | Arm straight at side, max extension; ideally slight hyperextension | Overhead-position security; lockout safety |
| **Mobility — shoulders** | Supine, knees bent, low back flat, lift straight arms overhead; should touch floor without arching | Snatch/jerk overhead position |
| **Mobility — T-spine** | Visual on standing posture (hyperkyphosis); jerk-grip behind-neck press as functional test | Bar-path safety in pull, overhead position |
| **Mobility — hips** | Full front + back squat with proper back arch | Pulling start position + receiving positions |
| **Mobility — ankles** | Bent-knee dorsiflexion; squat with heel-elevation test (¼" makes a difference = ankle limited) | Receiving position depth + balance |
| **Lift performance + ratios** | Snatch, C&J, FS, BS, press maxima if known | Strength imbalances to address |
| **Vertical jump (CMJ + from squat)** | Standard CMJ + jump from static squat bottom (both measured) | Explosiveness + reactivity diagnostic |
| **Grip strength (dynamometer)** | Best of 3 each hand | Pulling base; grip is foundational for snatch |
| **Trunk stability (plank)** | Hold proper plank; ≥1 min baseline | Positional strength under load |
| **Motor learning ability** | Finger-tapping test (10 sec, dominant hand, 5 trials averaged) OR observed lift learning rate over first week | Predicts how fast competition-lift technique will install |
| **Injury history + ROM limitations** | Interview + targeted ROM checks | What substitutions/contraindications govern programming |

**Skill classification** (Catalyst Table 33.4 — used to gate program design):

| Level | Description | Programming implication |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | No established technique. Learning phase. | Technique-focus only; subjective intensity; high GPP. |
| **1** | Beginner with established technique foundation. | 3-4 days/wk; 4-6 wk macros; subjective intensity; high GPP. |
| **2** | High beginner → low intermediate. Some training experience. | 4-5 days/wk; objective % start to appear; more competition-lift specificity. |
| **3** | Advanced intermediate, US national borderline. | 5-6 days/wk; structured macros; mesocycle distinctions. |
| **4** | Lower US national. | 5-6+ days/wk; full periodization; possible twice-daily sessions. |
| **5** | Top US national. | 6 days/wk minimum; twice-daily common; very advanced periodization. |
| **6** | Mid-level world championship performance. | Full-time specialization; individualized programming. |
| **7** | Absolute best of the sport. | Beyond the scope of this guide. |

**In-block monitoring** (capture each session):
- Top set load (relative + absolute) for primary exercises
- Misses (count + cause: mistake vs. failure; see §10)
- Technical execution rating (subjective, coach observation)
- RPE / perceived difficulty (subjective)
- Bodyweight (daily, 7-day rolling avg)
- Subjective: sleep quality, training enthusiasm, joint pain ratings
- For peaking: vertical jump or grip strength (most useful objective recovery proxies for strength athletes)

## 4. Session structure

**Ordering rule (and why):** Technically complex + high-CNS movements first while neurally fresh; pulling-pattern day typically followed by pushing-pattern day to vary movement demands. The competition lifts themselves come before pulls before squats, with squats often last because they create the most pulling-position interference if done first. Olympic lifts and Olympic pulls performed with maximal concentric speed regardless of load — *compensatory acceleration* (Hatfield) is default.

**Session template (canonical 3-6 exercise structure):**

1. **Warm-up** — full mobility/general warm-up (see §9) + 5 min empty-bar practice of the day's competition lift
2. **Primary competition lift** (snatch, clean, C&J, or close variant) — typically 4-6 working sets of 1-3 reps
3. **Secondary competition lift / variant** — block lifts, hang lifts, complexes, or partial movements; 3-5 sets
4. **Pulling variation** (snatch pull, clean pull, RDL, segment pull) — 3-5 sets of 2-5 reps
5. **Squat variation** (back squat, front squat, pause variants) — 3-5 sets of 1-6 reps
6. **Accessory** — pressing, jerk drills, ab/back, mobility post-session; lower priority

**Rest periods** (per Catalyst):

| Exercise type | Rest |
|---|---|
| Competition lifts (low rep) | 3-5 min |
| High-intensity squats / pulls | 3-5 min |
| Moderate squats / dynamic work | 2-3 min |
| Accessory (super-set / compound) | 30 sec – 1 min |
| On-the-minute (OTM) competition lifts | 60-90 sec (special protocol) |
| Maximal singles (true 1RM tests) | 5-15 min |

**Typical duration:** 60-120 min per session depending on level. Beginners 60-75 min; intermediates 75-90; advanced often 90-120 single-session or split into 2 daily sessions of 45-75 min each.

**Frequency:** 3-6 days/week + GPP/recovery activity on off days. Bulgarian variants go higher (multiple daily sessions, 12+ training units/week) but only for adapted advanced lifters.

## 5. Exercise selection

> See `exercise-selection.md` in this folder for the full filtered catalog with Catalyst-style annotations.

**Priority hierarchy** (Catalyst Table 34.8):

| Priority | Category | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **1 — Always present** | Competition lifts | Snatch, Clean & Jerk |
| **2 — Foundational strength** | Squats + pulling variants | Back squat, front squat, snatch pull, clean pull |
| **3 — Competition lift variants** | Hang, block, complex versions | Hang snatch, block clean, snatch + OHS complex |
| **4 — Overhead + pressing** | Speed and stability for catches | Push press, jerk, snatch push press, snatch balance |
| **5 — Technique remediators** | Selected by athlete's specific need | Tall snatch, muscle snatch, drop snatch, segment lifts |
| **6 — Accessory** | Ab/back, bodybuilding | Hanging leg raise, back extensions, T-bar rows, curls |

**Allowed accessories** (kept minimal, never compete with primary work for recovery):
- Trunk: hanging leg raise, weighted plank, back extension (3×10-15)
- Posterior chain: stiff-legged deadlift, RDL, good morning
- Pressing variants for shoulder/triceps: DB press (neutral grip), strict press
- Pulling endurance: chin-ups, weighted pull-ups, barbell row
- Mobility: see §9

**Substitution map** (Train-relevant — derived from Catalyst's "if available do X, otherwise Y" guidance):

| If unavailable / contraindicated | Substitute | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Front rack catch (wrist injury) | **Hang variant Olympic lifts** + power versions | Avoids loaded front rack; Andy's wrist solution |
| Overhead barbell press (shoulder injury) | DB neutral-grip press / landmine press | Removes the impingement angle |
| Behind-neck press / jerk | Front-rack push press only | Same reason |
| Full snatch (mobility limit) | Power snatch or split snatch | Avoids deep OH squat |
| Full clean (mobility limit) | Power clean or split clean | Avoids deep front squat |
| Heavy deadlift (back issue) | Snatch / clean pull from blocks at knee | Reduces lumbar moment from floor |
| Jerk dip (knee issue) | Push press to overhead | Removes the dip-and-recover demand |
| Olympic lifts entirely (joint injury) | Sled push, jumps, plyos for power; squats + presses for strength | Preserves training adaptation pathway |

**Disallowed in this style** (with reason):
- High-rep competition-lift sets (>3 reps) at near-maximal — neurological fatigue degrades speed; Catalyst keeps Oly lifts to 1-3 reps almost always
- Slow / paused concentric on competition lifts — defeats the speed-strength purpose
- Excessive variation per cycle — Catalyst's view is "rotate exercises across mesocycles, not within"; constant exercise churn prevents adaptation tracking
- Pure powerlifting low-bar mechanics for the squat — high-bar squat patterns transfer better to Olympic positions
- Bodybuilding-style isolation work as primary training — fine as accessory, not as block focus
- Endurance / metcon-style conditioning — directly opposed by the SAID principle (would compete for adaptation)

## 6. Periodization

**Model used:** Block periodization with linear macrocycles. Phases: General Preparation → Specific Preparation → Competition (peak). Optional Transition mesocycle between cycles.

**Why this model fits:** Olympic weightlifting demands extremely high specificity at competition, but the foundation of strength and technique requires variation. Block periodization lets you accumulate (preparation), intensify (specific prep), and realize (competition) without trying to develop everything at once — a direct application of Bompa/Issurin's logic, but expressed through Catalyst's exercise hierarchy.

**Macrocycle structure (typical lengths):**

| Tier | Macrocycle duration | Mesocycle structure |
|---|---|---|
| Beginner / Level 0-1 | 4-8 weeks | 1-2 mesocycles, no sharp distinction between prep and competition |
| Intermediate / Level 2-3 | 8-12 weeks | 2-3 mesocycles, clear prep → competition pattern |
| Advanced / Level 4-5 | 12-20 weeks | 3-4 mesocycles, full prep → specific prep → competition → transition |

**Standard mesocycle = 4 weeks** (range 3-6). Within each:

| Phase | Volume trend | Intensity trend | Exercise selection |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Preparation (Prep)** | High to highest | Lower to moderate | Strength lifts + Oly variants emphasized; competition lifts present but not peak intensity |
| **Specific preparation** | Moderate | Moderate to high | Competition lifts + close variants dominate; strength lifts maintained |
| **Competition (peak)** | Lowest | Highest | Competition lifts at near-max singles; strength lifts maintenance only |
| **Transition** (optional) | Very low | Very low / GPP | Active recovery, GPP, technique reset before next macro |

**Within-mesocycle (microcycle) modulation:**
- 4-week volume distribution (% of total): **30 / 27 / 23 / 20** (descending) for standard prep mesocycle
- Alternative: **30 / 23 / 27 / 20** (high-low-high-deload) for very high-volume cycles, gives better recovery between the two heaviest weeks
- Sustained high-volume option: **27 / 27 / 27 / 19** when total volume is moderate and a simple intensity progression carries the cycle
- Competition mesocycle: **35 / 28 / 22 / 15** — sharper taper

**Within-week (microcycle) modulation:**
- 6-day week typically has 2-3 genuinely heavy days, 2-3 lighter days
- Alternate or use 2H+1L patterns
- Pulling day typically followed by pushing day (movement-pattern recovery)
- Heavy squat day spaced ≥48h from highest-CNS power day if possible

**Progression rules:**
- *Within-block:* increase load when all prescribed reps clean; hold when borderline; back off when technique breaks down or speed drops noticeably
- *Between-block:* same macrocycle skeleton can be repeated with **higher absolute intensities** even if maxima haven't been retested — repeating an identical cycle with identical loads guarantees stagnation
- Mesocycle-to-mesocycle: rotate variants (e.g., snatch pull → segment snatch pull → snatch pull from riser) to introduce variation without sacrificing exercise hierarchy
- Across macrocycles: optimal volume grows over a lifter's career until reaching their individual ceiling, then plateaus, then declines with aging

**Deload position:** week 4 of every 4-week mesocycle is the lower-volume week per the distributions above. Catalyst does *not* prescribe a complete-rest deload — even the back-off week contains real training; the burden simply drops by ~30-50%.

## 7. Sample programs

> Pulled from Catalyst Sample Training Programs (pp. 806+). Skill Level 0 and 1 templates included as starting points. Levels 2-5 require individualization beyond the scope of a template.

### Skill Level 0 — Learning Phase (12 weeks, 3 days/week + GPP)

> For new athletes with no established technique. Goal: develop a usable technical foundation before any strength-focused programming.

**Structure:** 4-week blocks; technique-focused, subjective loading.

**Weeks 1-4** (uniform weight throughout for technique consolidation):

```
Day 1
  Hang Power Snatch (knee)              — sets of 5 reps, light
  Hang Snatch (knee)                    — sets of 5 reps, light
  Segment Snatch Deadlift (1", knee, mid-thigh)
  Squat                                  — back squat

Day 2
  Power Jerk
  Press
  Snatch Press + Overhead Squat (complex)
  Squat                                  — front squat

Day 3
  Hang Power Clean (knee)
  Hang Clean (knee) + Power Jerk (complex)
  Segment Clean Deadlift (1", knee, mid-thigh)
  Squat                                  — back squat

(Final session of W4)
  Hang Snatch (knee)
  Hang Clean (knee)
  Power Jerk
  Front Squat
```

**Weeks 5-8** (transition to from-floor lifts):

```
Day 1: Power Snatch  /  Hang Snatch (knee)  /  Segment Snatch Pull (knee) + Snatch Pull  /  Squat
Day 2: Split Jerk    /  Push Press           /  Snatch Push Press + OHS                  /  Squat
Day 3: Power Clean   /  Hang Clean (knee) + Power Jerk  /  Segment Clean Pull (knee) + Clean Pull  /  Squat
```

**Weeks 9-11** (full competition lifts emerge):

```
Day 1: Power Snatch  /  Snatch        /  Snatch Pull   /  Squat
Day 2: Power Clean   /  Split Jerk    /  Push Press    /  Squat
Day 3: Clean & Jerk  /  Clean Pull    /  Snatch Push Press + OHS  /  Squat
```

**Week 12** — Test snatch + clean & jerk to establish baseline maxima.

**GPP each week** (alongside lifts): sprints (high-knees + standing/walking start sprints), jumps (standing LJ, vertical, running LJ), throws (medicine ball, shot put), carries (farmer's walk, overhead carry), cross-country 1-2 km, sports/games 15-20 min. Spread across the week.

### Skill Level 1 — Beginner Development (4-6 week macros, 3-4 days/week)

> For lifters with established basic technique. Subjective intensity; coach-controlled in the gym.

**4-week sample block:**

```
WEEK 1
Monday    — Snatch Skill Work  /  Snatch 5×3  /  Snatch Pull 3×5  /  Back Squat 3×8  /  Back ext + Abs
Tuesday   — Clean Skill Work  /  Clean & Jerk 5×(3+1)  /  Clean Pull 3×5  /  Push Press 3×5  /  Back ext + Abs  /  GPP (sprints, jumps, carries)
Thursday  — Snatch Skill Work  /  Power Snatch 5×3  /  Snatch PP + OHS 4×(5+1)  /  Front Squat 3×5  /  Back ext + Abs
Saturday  — Jerk Skill Work  /  Power Clean & Power Jerk 5×(3+1)  /  Clean Pull 3×5  /  Back Squat 4×5  /  Back ext + Abs  /  GPP (throws, jumps, x-country)

WEEK 2
Monday    — Power Snatch + Hang Snatch (knee) 5×(1+1)  /  Halting Snatch DL (mid-thigh) 3×3  /  Back Squat 4×5
Tuesday   — Power Snatch 5×2  /  Snatch PP + OHS 3×(5+1)  /  Front Squat 3×5  /  GPP (sprints, jumps, games)
Thursday  — Power Clean + Hang Clean (knee) 5×(1+1)  /  Halting Clean DL (mid-thigh) 3×3  /  Push Press 3×5
Saturday  — Power Clean & Power Jerk 5×(1+3)  /  Snatch Pull 3×4  /  Back Squat 4×5  /  GPP

(Weeks 3-4 follow same shape with variation; final week reduces volume + intensity ~25% for recovery before next block)
```

## 8. Technique

> Olympic weightlifting is technique-driven. This section is short — exhaustive technical instruction is the bulk of the book itself (pp. 15–591). This is the agent's quick reference for what matters at the programming level.

### The three-pull model (snatch + clean)

| Phase | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Preparatory position** | Relaxed position at bar before lift; final mental setup |
| **Starting position** | Last position before bar leaves floor; correct posture must be set |
| **1st pull** | Floor → mid-thigh; legs drive against ground; weight shifts slightly back over feet |
| **2nd pull** | Mid-thigh → full extension; includes double knee bend (scoop); maximal hip + knee extension |
| **3rd pull** | Active pull under bar with arms while body relocates downward; ends at receiving position |
| **Receiving position** | Bar overhead (snatch) or on shoulders (clean); deep squat for full lifts |
| **Recovery** | Stand from receiving position |

### Jerk phases

Preparatory → starting → **dip** → **drive** → **push under** → receiving (split typically) → recovery

### Force application principles (Newton's laws operationalized)

- **1st + 2nd pull:** lifter applies force against the *ground* (with feet) to elevate bar
- **3rd pull:** lifter applies force against the *bar* (with arms) to relocate body downward
- The transition is the critical inflection point — pressure shifts from feet to hands; in a proficient lift, it's a single continuous action
- **Center of mass / line of gravity / center of pressure** must balance over the front edge of the heel area throughout the lift, with the understanding that center of pressure and line of gravity *diverge* during fast movements (e.g., pressure shifts to balls of feet during ankle extension while line of gravity stays back)

### Strength vs. technique

Neither compensates for the other past a threshold. Technique is the medium through which strength is expressed. The weakest link limits the lift. Program design must address both — never assume strength alone will fix a technique problem (it won't) or that technique work alone will fix a strength problem (also won't).

### Legs vs. hips

Both knee and hip extension are critical. Knee extension primarily drives bar *elevation*; hip extension primarily drives bar *speed*. Lifters with different proportions will naturally rely on each to different degrees (long-femur → more hip; short-femur → more knee), but both must be maximally trained.

### Universal technical cues

Catalyst's technique cues are extensive (see §16-19 in source). Key reminders for the planning agent:
- "Push the floor away" → leg drive into pull
- "Patience off the floor" / "Smooth off the floor" → controls premature 2nd pull
- "Elbows high and to the sides" → 3rd pull mechanics; prevents bar swing forward
- "Stay over the bar" → posture in 1st pull; prevents early hip rise
- "Hit high (thigh)" → timing of 2nd pull initiation
- "Trust the legs" / "Jump it up" → counters premature arm bend

## 9. Recovery & nutrition

> Common slot: `concepts/recovery.md` for universal floors. Style-specific additions below.

**Style-specific recovery demands:**

- **Sleep is non-negotiable**: minimum 7-8h adults, 9.5h+ for youth athletes. 7.5-8h vs. ideal can reduce work capacity ~30% (Dvorkin). Schedule sleep with the same priority as training.
- **CNS recovery** is the gating constraint after heavy snatch/CJ days, not muscular recovery. Plan ≥48h between maximum-effort competition-lift sessions (exception: Bulgarian-style adapted lifters).
- **Heavy squat day spacing**: ≥48h before a peak-power day for the same reason.
- **2-3h buffer between training end and bedtime** — CNS needs time to wind down.
- **Hand care** is a daily ongoing practice (sand calluses smooth, moisturize nightly with Cornhuskers or similar; treat blisters before they tear) — torn hands halt training.
- **Joint preservation** for high-volume training: tendon strengthening requires high-rep low-intensity work periodically (good morning, light squat sets of 8-15 in early prep blocks) since tendons adapt slower than muscle.

**Restorative modalities** (rotate to avoid accommodation):

| Modality | When | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Contrast hydrotherapy (hot/cold) | 3-5×/wk | 5-10 min hot / 2-3 min cold; start in cold if sore, in heat if stiff |
| Cold plunge | Post heavy session | 50-60°F, 10-15 min |
| Hot tub | Evenings | Body temp drop after exit aids sleep |
| Dry / steam sauna | Variable | Stay hydrated; can alternate with cold for contrast |
| Massage | 1-3×/wk ideal, ≥1×/month minimum | Avoid deep tissue within 7 days of competition |
| Stretching + SMR | Daily | Especially post-session and pre-bed |
| Light swimming | Rest days | Decompression + low-impact movement |
| Napping | Daily 20-30 min if possible | Critical for multi-session days |
| Local cryotherapy | Acute / chronic local pain | Max 10-15 min, no more than hourly |

**Overtraining signs** (track for early intervention):
- Chronic fatigue, insomnia, elevated resting HR / BP
- Reduced speed, reduced grip strength, reduced vertical jump
- Increased joint soreness, irritability, GI distress
- Inability to perform prescribed training that was previously manageable

When suspected: reduce intensity + volume significantly; possibly enforce 1+ wk complete rest; resume with very light loads and gradual ramp.

**Nutritional priorities for this style:**
- Adequate protein (universal: 0.8-1g/lb baseline)
- Carbs to support glycogen replenishment, especially around training (Olympic lifts are glycolytic + CNS-demanding)
- Bodyweight management for weight class — Catalyst's stance: don't cut weight if athlete is already lean; manage body composition first
- Avoid sleep-inducing drugs (degrade sleep quality); consider cortisol-modulating supplements (holy basil) if stress is high

**In-season modifications** (not really applicable — weightlifting *is* the in-season activity for most athletes who use it — but for athletes using these lifts as part of a multi-sport program, reduce volume and frequency, prioritize technique sessions, drop Bulgarian-style loading entirely).

## 10. Coach's notes

Tacit knowledge from Everett's *Training Practices* and *Mental Game* chapters that doesn't fit other sections:

- **The 1 kg rule.** When approaching PR territory, set up to make a +1 kg PR *first*. A 1 kg PR beats repeated failed attempts at +5. Adjust warm-up jumps so the final attempt isn't an arbitrary +5: e.g., from 100 kg, take 100-105-110-114-118-122-126 instead of 100-105-110-115-120-126. Decreasing jumps as you approach PR weight reduces fatigue and increases successful-attempt confidence.

- **Misses count as practice.** Some misses per cycle are expected; *no misses* suggests training is too easy. But missing can become a habit — coach must decide before each session what misses warrant retry and what trigger a stop or weight drop. If a lifter is consistently missing prescriptions, the prescription is wrong (too heavy, too many reps, or wrong exercise for that day).

- **Mistake vs. failure.** After a miss, diagnose: was it a *correctable technical mistake* or *physical failure*? Mistakes → repeat with the correction. Failure → stop or drop weight. Repeated misses without progress → move on; the program absorbs the missed reps elsewhere.

- **Reduce weight to finish exercises on a make.** Whenever possible, end on a successful lift even if it means dropping load substantially.

- **Warm-up rep policy.** Beginners do full prescribed reps on all warm-up sets (technique repetition = adaptation). Advanced lifters can do partials to conserve energy for working sets — but only when goal is testing maximal effort, not training adaptation.

- **Barbell loading order.** Heaviest plates innermost, descending outward. Don't mix bumper-plate brands side-to-side (unequal bounce damages plates + bar). Use collars whenever sliding is even slightly possible. Load/unload alternating sides to prevent tipping.

- **Record keeping is non-negotiable.** A training journal that records sets/reps/loads + subjective notes (sleep, energy, soreness, joint pain) is what makes individualization possible over the long term. Memory is too unreliable.

- **The mental game.** Athletes should know their next workout before they walk in. Visualization — at the level of detail of every sensation (sound of plates, smell of chalk, feel of bar in hand) — is a trainable skill that builds confidence. Coaches: balance praise and critique (every session has at least one positive); use positive-action cues ("chest up") not negation cues ("don't let hips rise").

- **Positivity is operational, not personality.** Reframe negative experiences into specific action items written in the journal. "Snatch felt off" → useless. "Next time: pull elbows up and out aggressively on every rep" → useful.

- **Don't socialize during training.** Especially during another lifter's set. Eye contact with a lifting athlete is taboo. Train atmosphere is created by the coach and reinforced by the team.

- **Bumper plates and racks are expensive.** Train athletes in equipment care from day one. Back away from the squat rack before lifting. Don't drop empty bars. Set bars down in racks under control. Keep lifting area clear of trip hazards.

- **Athletes choose their starting age — coaches mostly don't.** Don't apply youth-development protocols to adult learners; the time scale is compressed and the program must be remedial in many respects (mobility, GPP, technique acquisition all happen at once).

- **Women's training is fundamentally the same as men's.** Differences are hormonal (lower testosterone → lower neurological ceiling, slightly higher rep capacity at given %, manageable timing of menstrual cycle for competition if desired). Coaching communication may need more praise-balance for some athletes — but never coddle.

- **Masters athletes have to accept reduced capacity.** Failure to do so is a primary injury vector. Often 1-2 heavy days/wk + 1-2 light, possibly capped at 80-85% for most "heavy" work, with maxes reserved for meet prep.

- **The Bulgarian method works — for advanced lifters with high strength bases.** Daily maxes work because the athletes are snatching/CJing weights that aren't a huge % of their *absolute* strength. For a weaker lifter, daily-max competition lifts represent a much larger systemic demand and tend to break the athlete instead of building them.

## 11. Programming decision tree

The runbook a planning agent follows to design a macrocycle in this style:

### Step 1 — Classify the athlete
- **Inputs:** training age, biological age, technique status (assessment §3), mobility status, injury history, available frequency, competition schedule, prior strength bases
- **Decision:** Skill Level 0-5 (Catalyst Table 33.4)
- **Tier-specific gates:**
  - Level 0 → Learning Phase template (§7); no objective % loading; subjective intensity only
  - Level 1 → 4-6 wk macros, 3-4 days/wk, subjective intensity, high GPP
  - Level 2-3 → 8-12 wk macros, 4-5 days/wk, % loading starts to dominate
  - Level 4-5 → 12-20 wk macros, 5-6+ days/wk, possible 2× daily; full periodization

### Step 2 — Pick the macrocycle structure
- **Inputs:** Skill level + days until next significant competition (or testing date)
- **Decision rules:**
  - Time to comp < 6 wk → single competition mesocycle
  - 6-12 wk → 1 prep + 1 competition
  - 12-16 wk → 2 prep + 1 competition
  - 16-20 wk → 2-3 prep + 1 competition + transition
- **Bulgarian variant:** consider only for advanced lifters with strong squat-to-CJ ratio and prior adaptation to high frequency

### Step 3 — Lay down exercise scheduling
- **Inputs:** Macro structure + identified weaknesses from assessment + competition schedule
- **Decisions** (in this order):
  1. Place squat days (typically 3×/wk for prep, 2×/wk for competition mesos)
  2. Place pulling days (1-2×/wk; cluster with the squat day they best support)
  3. Place primary competition-lift days (snatch focus + CJ focus + combined; usually 2-3 of each per week)
  4. Layer technique work and remediation exercises into lighter days
  5. Add overhead/pressing work, accessories
- **Cap exercises at 3-6 per session** (Catalyst general rule)
- **Movement pattern alternation:** pulling-emphasis day followed by pushing-emphasis day

### Step 4 — Set volume and intensity

| Component | Building volume (prep) | Maintenance volume (comp) |
|---|---|---|
| Competition lifts | 8-15 reps/wk per lift @ 70-85% | 5-10 reps/wk per lift @ 80-95% |
| Squat | 15-30 reps/wk @ 75-90% | 5-15 reps/wk @ 80-90% |
| Pulling | 10-20 reps/wk @ 85-110% snatch/clean | 5-10 reps/wk @ 85-100% |
| Pressing | 10-20 reps/wk | 5-10 reps/wk |
| Accessory | as tolerated | minimal |

- Distribute weekly volume per mesocycle pattern (30-27-23-20% standard)
- Within-week: 2-3 heavy days + 2-3 lighter days (or 2H+1L pattern)
- Push for compensatory acceleration (maximal concentric speed) on all primary work
- For Skill Level 0-1: use **subjective** intensity ("heavy", "moderate", "light") — % prescriptions are inaccurate when maxes are unstable

### Step 5 — Define progression
- **Within-block:** add 1-2.5% per session on primary lifts when sets clean; hold when borderline; back off if technique breaks or speed drops
- **Between-mesocycle:** rotate exercise variants (segment → halting → from-blocks); slightly higher absolute intensities; same exercise hierarchy
- **Between-macrocycle:** re-test maxes; recalibrate %; rotate emphasis (e.g., squat-emphasis cycle if squat lagging)
- **Deload position:** week 4 of each mesocycle (~30-50% volume reduction)
- **Wave-loading option** for intermediate+: 80-83-86 / 82-85-88 / 84-87-90 across reps or sets to build confidence and overload

## 12. Common mistakes

Catalyst's *Universal Errors* chapter (pp. 491-531) is a masterclass — these are the high-frequency mistakes that programming + coaching must catch:

1. **Leading with the hips (1st pull).** Hips rise faster than shoulders. Caused by weak legs relative to hips, or rushing off the floor without postural control. Fix: posture-controlled deadlift variants (segment, halting, lift-off), then complexes pairing them with the snatch/clean. Cues: "Chest up off the floor," "Smooth off the floor."

2. **Jumping forward.** Forward imbalance somewhere in the lift. Causes: shoulders too far in front of bar, failure to shift weight back in 1st pull, swinging bar forward in 2nd pull, excessive hip extension. Multiple subcases; fixes vary. Non-specific fix: rubber mat barrier in front of toes or PVC pipe in front of bar end.

3. **Bar swinging forward in 2nd pull.** Excessive hip extension (hips through the bar instead of straight up), inadequate leg drive, bar too far from body before contact, stiff arms. Fix: dip snatch/clean, snatch/clean from power position, pulling complexes emphasizing close proximity.

4. **Premature arm bend.** Arms bend in 1st/2nd pull instead of staying passive. Causes: forward imbalance, too far over the bar, premature 2nd pull initiation, grip too narrow, lack of confidence, grip too tight, under-extension. Multiple sub-fixes. Cue: "Trust the legs, jump it up."

5. **Slow 3rd pull.** Improper mechanics (elbows don't pull high and out), bar too far from body, lack of aggression in turnover. Fix: tall snatch, muscle snatch, high-pull variants. Cue: "Elbows high and out."

6. **Pushing bar too far back toward hips.** Over-correction of close-bar cue. Bar moves behind mid-foot, creating compensatory forward rock. Fix: halting/segment pulls focusing on subtle backward shift, not aggressive push-back.

7. **Bar moving straight up past knees** (instead of curving back into hip). Bar drifts away from body; lifter often unconsciously avoids body contact. Fix: hang variants from above the knee, segment cleans pausing at knee, deadlift to power position.

8. **Jumping backward (excessive).** Weight too far back in pull or excessive hip extension. Differs from feet-only sweeping backward (which has different cause — usually incomplete leg drive or improper foot-relocation mechanics in 3rd pull).

9. **Feet sweeping backward.** Looks like a backward jump but only feet move. Bar/body stay in original area = same effect as bar forward. Caused by incomplete leg drive or improper foot lift (knees bend to lift feet instead of squatting motion). Cues: "Land flat-footed," "Land on the heels."

10. **Early scoop / premature 2nd pull.** Knees move forward before bar reaches mid-thigh, killing speed and shifting balance forward. Causes: forward imbalance, postural weakness, intentional but incorrect double knee bend, lack of confidence. Fix: halting deadlifts at mid-thigh, segment snatches/cleans pausing at mid-thigh. Cue: "Hit high before you explode."

11. **Variation churn within a cycle** (programming mistake). Changing exercises every week prevents adaptation tracking. Catalyst's rule: keep exercises uniform for 3-4 weeks at a time within a mesocycle; rotate variants between mesocycles.

12. **Excessive volume relative to recovery capacity.** More common than excessive intensity. If a lifter is breaking down, reduce volume *before* reducing intensity — volume is the harder demand on recovery (with the Bulgarian counter-example of low-volume + max-intensity working for adapted advanced lifters).

13. **Identical macrocycle repetition.** Repeating a program with identical absolute loads guarantees no improvement. Repeat the structure, increase the absolute weights — even by 1 kg.

14. **Over-grinding singles in training.** "Maximal effort without significant psychological arousal" is the training-day rule (per Catalyst): leave 2-3 kg in the tank vs. competition-arousal max. Continually arousing to true max in training is a fast path to overtraining and stagnation.

15. **Treating recovery as optional.** Athletes obsess over training stimulus and ignore restoration. No amount of programming compensates for chronic under-sleeping or skipped post-session recovery work.

## 13. Style interactions

> Required because Train athletes typically combine this style with at least one other. Catalyst chapters on Specific Populations + general programming guidance + Train-context inference below.

**Day-pairing rules** (Catalyst principles + Train-specific):
- Heavy squat day must precede any jump-emphasis day by **≥48h** (per [[vertical-jump]] §13 and Catalyst CNS-spacing guidance)
- Olympic-power day (snatch / CJ at intensity) needs **≥48h** before another high-CNS day in the same movement pattern
- Pulling-emphasis day naturally pairs with pushing-emphasis day next (movement pattern recovery)
- Avoid scheduling deep mobility / loaded stretch within 24-48h before a jump or peak-power day — saps adductor / hamstring force (per [[vertical-jump]])

**Recovery overlap with other styles:**
- **Shares CNS budget with:** sprinting, jump training ([[vertical-jump]]), powerlifting, heavy plyometrics. Limit to one of these per day; sequence to allow recovery between.
- **Doesn't conflict with:** mobility, light bodybuilding accessories, low-intensity cardio (rowing/cycling at recovery pace), swimming as recovery.
- **Conflicts with:** endurance training (long-distance running, traditional cardio), high-volume CrossFit-style metcons, sport-specific volume that includes heavy lower-body demand.

**Calorie / load conflicts:**
- Olympic training is *moderate-high* caloric demand — not as high as endurance work, but the CNS-recovery component means glycogen replenishment matters more than gross caloric load
- Cutting weight: Catalyst's stance is preserve strength first; if already lean, don't cut. For Train arcs that include a body composition goal, **time the cut into preparation mesocycles** (not competition mesos) and **never during a peak phase** (matches [[vertical-jump]] §9 "no cut Block 3" rule)

**Equipment / facility conflicts:**
- Requires platform + bumper plates + sufficient ceiling clearance + Olympic bar with bearings
- Cannot share platform with another lifter doing concurrent work (safety + flow)
- Needs blocks/jerk blocks for higher-skill programming (block lifts, dropped jerks)

**Compatible co-styles:**
- **[[vertical-jump]]** — extremely compatible. The Olympic lifts ARE jump training (per VJ Bible §2.1 strength-speed and speed-strength categories). Catalyst's snatch and clean serve double duty for explosive power expression. Day-pairing rules above govern.
- **[[dylan-shannon]]** — compatible for upper-body day pairing. Dylan's pull-up / dip / bench protocols can occupy upper-emphasis days without competing for lower-body CNS. Hang-variant Olympic lifts work well as Dylan's secondary lower-power inputs.
- **General strength** (squat / deadlift / press progressions) — natural fit; Catalyst integrates these as foundational strength layer below the competition lifts.
- **Mobility / flexibility** (independent specialist work) — required as ongoing support, not really a competing style.
- **GPP** (jumps, sprints, throws, carries) — Catalyst explicitly recommends as foundation work for Skill Level 0-1 and as periodic re-introduction for advanced lifters returning from layoffs.

**Incompatible co-styles:**
- **Hypertrophy-emphasis bodybuilding** with high-rep / high-volume protocols — competes for recovery and drives sarcoplasmic vs. myofibrillar hypertrophy (Catalyst calls this "counterproductive — increases bodyweight without proportional strength")
- **Endurance / long-distance running / sport-of-the-week conditioning** — direct SAID conflict; suppresses speed-strength adaptation
- **High-volume CrossFit / metcons** — same reason, with added high-fatigue Olympic-lift reps that degrade technique
- **Pure powerlifting** (low-bar squat, sumo deadlift, paused bench focus) — different bar paths and positions; mixing the two waters down both. Pick one as primary; do the other as maintenance only.
