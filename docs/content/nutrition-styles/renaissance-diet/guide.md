# Renaissance Diet — Nutrition Style Guide

> **Purpose.** This document helps nutritionists — human or AI — design diet plans using the Renaissance Diet 2.0 methodology. Read by the nutritionist specialist when designing or adjusting an athlete's nutrition during any phase. The bar is "another nutritionist can pick this up and run a diet from it."

---

*An evidence-based nutritional periodization style for athletes pursuing body composition or performance goals, organized around a strict hierarchy of importance: calorie balance > macros > timing > food composition > supplements.*

---

## 1. When to use this style

**Goal types this serves:**
- Fat loss (cutting) while preserving lean mass
- Muscle gain (lean bulking) without excess fat
- Maintenance phases (between cut/bulk cycles)
- Performance peaking for sport competition
- Long-term body composition management

**Athlete profile fit:**
- Training age: any (novice → elite)
- Equipment minimum: a scale, a way to track weight, optionally a macro-tracking app (MacroFactor, MyFitnessPal)
- Time required: ~10 min/day during precision phases (tracking); 0 min during loose maintenance
- Compatible co-styles: any training style — RD is the meta-framework, not training-specific

**Contraindications (do NOT recruit this style if):**
- The athlete has a documented eating disorder history — RD's tracking emphasis can re-trigger
- Pregnancy/nursing — defer to medical guidance, not aggressive periodization
- Severely under-resourced athlete (no food scale, can't weigh in regularly) — RD's data dependency makes it brittle without baseline measurement
- Athlete explicitly rejects tracking — RD without measurement is just generic eating advice

## 2. Mechanism — how this style produces results

**Lead.** Renaissance Diet works because it forces the athlete to prioritize the levers that actually move body composition (calories and protein) over the levers that don't (timing, food choices, supplements). The result: most of the cognitive load goes to what matters, very little goes to what doesn't.

### The Hierarchy of Importance (the load-bearing concept)

```
            ┌────────────────────────┐
            │ 5. Supplements (~5%)   │
            ├────────────────────────┤
            │ 4. Food composition    │
            │    (~5%)               │
            ├────────────────────────┤
            │ 3. Nutrient timing     │
            │    (~10%)              │
            ├────────────────────────┤
            │ 2. Macros (~30%)       │
            ├────────────────────────┤
            │ 1. Calorie balance     │
            │    (~50%)              │
            └────────────────────────┘
```

The percentages are heuristic, not literal. The principle: **don't optimize layer N+1 until layer N is dialed in.** Most diet failures invert this — obsessing over supplements while calories are off, or stressing meal timing while protein is under target.

### Caloric Constraint Hypothesis (CCH)

Macros aren't independent variables. Because each gram of macro carries fixed kcal (P:4, C:4, F:9), increasing one *forces* decreasing others to hold total calories. This is why "high-carb" and "high-fat" diets can both work for fat loss — calorie balance is the actual driver, macro split is the implementation detail.

**Implication:** when adjusting a plan, change calories first, then *redistribute* macros within the new total — don't add or subtract macros independently.

### Set points and settling points

The body has both a **set point** (genetic predisposition for body weight) and **settling point** (current habit-driven equilibrium). Set points shift slowly with sustained body composition change. Settling points shift quickly with habits.

**Implication:** weight loss plateaus are usually the body settling at a new equilibrium, not metabolic damage. Patience over panic.

### Phases (the 4 dieting states)

| Phase | Caloric state | Goal | Typical duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fat loss | Hypocaloric (deficit) | Lose body fat | 6–12 weeks |
| Muscle gain | Hypercaloric (surplus) | Build lean mass | 8–16 weeks |
| Post-diet maintenance | Isocaloric | Reset, prevent rebound | 2–4 weeks |
| Long-term maintenance | Isocaloric | Sustainable equilibrium | Ongoing |

Concurrent recomposition (simultaneous fat loss + muscle gain) is possible but inefficient outside novice/PED contexts. **Pick one direction per phase.**

### The 6-Construct Adherence Model

Adherence is what makes the diet work. Six factors compound:

1. **Inspiration** — the spark (event, photo, goal)
2. **Motivation** — drive in early weeks
3. **Intention** — explicit plan
4. **Discipline** — willpower on low-motivation days
5. **Habit** — automaticity over time (60–90 days)
6. **Passion** — sustained love for the process

Early-phase adherence runs on motivation + discipline. Long-term adherence runs on habit + passion. **A plan that ignores the adherence layer fails regardless of macro precision.**

## 3. Athlete assessment

**Baseline tests** (run at the start of any block in this style):

| Test | Protocol | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| 7-day rolling bodyweight | Daily fasted weigh-in for 7 days; take average | True starting weight (filters water/sodium noise) |
| Body fat estimate | Photos + DEXA if available; otherwise visual + waist circumference | Direction of body comp goal |
| Daily activity classification | See table below | Caloric demand baseline |
| Food preference inventory | Athlete lists foods they love, hate, and can prep | Adherence floor |
| Tracking ability | Has the athlete used MacroFactor / MFP before? | Whether to start with strict tracking or loose pattern |

**Daily activity classification:**

| Day type | Definition | Approx kcal burned in training |
|---|---|---|
| Non-training | No formal exercise | 0 |
| Light | Workouts ≤1 hour | ~300 |
| Moderate | 1–2 hour sessions | ~500 |
| Hard | >2 hour sessions, multi-modal | ~1000+ |

Most athletes have 2–4 distinct day types in a week — the diet plans for each.

**In-block monitoring** (what to capture each week):
- 7-day rolling avg bodyweight
- Lift performance (the canary — if dropping during a cut, calories go UP, not down)
- Sleep average and quality
- Subjective hunger/cravings
- Adherence (how many off-plan days)

## 4. Daily structure — meal architecture

**Ordering rule (and why):** spread protein across 4–8 meals to maximize muscle protein synthesis (MPS) windows. Carbs cluster around training. Fats fill in around the windows where carbs aren't needed.

**Daily template (5-meal example for a moderate-day athlete):**

1. **Breakfast** — ~25% daily P, low/moderate C, low F if pre-training
2. **Mid-morning** — ~20% daily P, moderate C, moderate F
3. **Pre-training (or mid-day if no training)** — ~20% daily P, *high C*, low F
4. **Post-training** — ~25% daily P, *highest C of the day*, low F
5. **Pre-bed** — ~10% daily P (slow-digesting if possible), low C, moderate F

**Typical meal frequency:** 4–6 meals/day. Below 4: protein doses too large to fully utilize MPS. Above 8: digestion overhead, no additional benefit.

**Frequency guideline:** spread protein every 3–5 hours during waking hours.

## 5. Food selection

**Macro hierarchy when choosing foods:**

1. **Hit protein first** — primary macro for body comp during deficits and surpluses alike
2. **Then carbs** — fuel for training, recovery, performance
3. **Then fats** — fill remaining calories; meet hormonal floor (~0.3 g/lb)

**Protein sources** (ranked by quality + practicality):

| Source | PDCAAS | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Whey, milk, dairy | 1.0 | Most digestible; whey for shakes |
| Eggs | 1.0 | Complete amino profile |
| Lean meat (chicken, lean beef, fish) | 0.92–1.0 | Foundation of athlete diets |
| Soy isolate | 1.0 | Best vegan option for completeness |
| Other plant proteins | <1.0 | Combine sources for completeness (rice + beans) |

**Carbohydrate sources** (matched to timing):

| Window | Preferred carbs |
|---|---|
| Pre-training (1–4h before) | Moderate-GI (oats, rice, fruit) |
| Intra-training (>1h sessions) | High-GI (gels, sports drinks, bananas) |
| Post-training (within 1h) | High-GI for fast glycogen replenishment (rice, fruit, dextrose) |
| Non-training meals | Low/moderate-GI, high fiber (vegetables, legumes, whole grains) |

**Fat sources:**

| Type | Priority | Sources |
|---|---|---|
| Monounsaturated | Highest | Olive oil, avocado, nuts |
| Polyunsaturated (omega-3) | High | Fatty fish, flax, chia |
| Saturated | Moderate | Eggs, dairy, lean meat — within total fat limit |
| Trans | Avoid | Industrial fats, margarine |

**Substitution map:**

| If unavailable / contraindicated | Substitute | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Animal protein (vegan) | Soy isolate + varied legumes/grains | Completeness through combination |
| Whey (lactose intolerant) | Pea/rice protein blend | Equivalent MPS at slightly higher dose |
| Solid pre-workout meal (time-constrained) | Liquid carbs + whey shake | Faster digestion, same macros |
| High-GI carbs (diabetic) | Slow-digesting carbs (oats, sweet potato) | Stable blood sugar |

**Disallowed in standing menu (with reason):**
- Daily alcohol consumption — hinders MPS, disrupts sleep, prioritized fuel pathway over fat metabolism
- Severe restriction of any macro — fat <0.3 g/lb tanks hormones; carbs at zero kills high-intensity training quality
- Crash protein-only diets — muscle loss inevitable without sufficient calorie support

## 6. Periodization

**Model used:** Block periodization with explicit phase transitions.

**Why this fits:** body composition adapts to caloric levels over weeks. Sudden jumps cause rebound. Gradual phases let the body settle at each new equilibrium.

**Phase structure:**

| Phase | Duration | Caloric state | Weight rate (target) | Protein |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I — Fat Loss | 6–12 weeks | Hypocaloric | −0.5 to −1.0% bodyweight/wk | 1.0–1.5 g/lb |
| II — Post-Cut Maintenance | 2–4 weeks | Isocaloric | Hold ±1.25% | 1.0 g/lb |
| III — Muscle Gain | 8–16 weeks | Hypercaloric | +0.25 to +0.5% bodyweight/wk | 0.8–1.0 g/lb |
| IV — Long-term Maintenance | Variable | Isocaloric | Hold ±1.25% | 0.7–1.0 g/lb |

**Single-phase weight loss limits:**
- ≤10% of total bodyweight per fat loss phase
- 3% per phase recommended for sustainability
- Faster loss = greater muscle loss risk

**Within-block progression rules:**
- **When to bump deficit:** weight stalled 2+ weeks AND lifts are holding AND sleep is good → drop 100–150 kcal
- **When to hold:** weight tracking on target, lifts holding, no flag signals → keep going
- **When to back off / refeed:** lifts dropping OR sleep degrading OR persistent fatigue → +200 kcal/day or 1-week refeed at maintenance
- **Mandatory refeed:** every 8 weeks of cutting, insert a 1-week maintenance break

**Between-block progression rules:**
- Cut → Maintenance: ramp +100 kcal every 4 days until at maintenance
- Maintenance → Bulk: similar ramp, +100 kcal every 4–7 days
- Never invert: don't go from deficit straight to surplus or vice versa without a maintenance bridge

## 7. Sample diet plans

### Cut template (intermediate athlete, ~190 lb, moderate training)

```
Maintenance estimate: 3000 kcal
Cut target:           2600 kcal (-400 kcal, ~0.75 lb/wk)

Macros:
  Protein: 190g (1.0 g/lb)
  Fat:     75g  (0.4 g/lb)
  Carbs:   285g (filling remaining calories)

Daily meal pattern (training day):
  Breakfast (8am):       50p / 60c / 15f
  Mid-morning (10am):    25p / 20c / 10f
  Pre-training (1pm):    45p / 80c / 15f
  Post-training (5pm):   45p / 90c / 10f
  Pre-bed (9pm):         25p / 35c / 25f

Rest day adjustments:
  Drop ~100g carbs across pre/post-training meals (move to fats)
  Same protein (190g)
```

### Maintenance template (post-cut, same athlete)

```
Maintenance:       3000 kcal
Macros:
  Protein: 190g (1.0 g/lb — keep high to maintain lean mass)
  Fat:     85g
  Carbs:   330g

Pattern: same 5-meal structure, slightly larger portions
Adherence: looser tracking (weekly weight, biweekly photos)
```

### Muscle gain template (lean bulk)

```
Surplus target:    3300 kcal (+10% over maintenance, ~0.4 lb/wk gain)
Macros:
  Protein: 190g (0.8–1.0 g/lb)
  Fat:     90g
  Carbs:   400g

Pattern: same 5 meals, larger carbs especially around training
Adherence: weekly weigh-ins, watch for fat gain >25% of total weight gain
```

### Endurance event prep (carb-loading, optional)

For events >3 hours: increase carbs to 3–5 g/lb bodyweight over 2–3 days pre-event. Fiber lowered. Glycogen-saturated state at race start.

## 8. Tracking and adjustment mechanics

This is where most amateur dieters fail. RD's edge is the explicit tracking-then-adjusting loop.

### Weigh-in protocol

- Daily fasted weigh-in (same scale, same time, same conditions)
- Calculate **7-day rolling average** — that's the signal; daily numbers are noise
- Track in MacroFactor, a spreadsheet, or a simple notes file

### Adjustment cadence

**Don't adjust week-to-week.** Wait for 2–3 weeks of data, then make ONE change at a time.

| Trend (over 2–3 weeks) | Action |
|---|---|
| Weight tracking on goal rate, lifts holding | Hold — repeat |
| Weight slower than goal, lifts holding | Drop 100–150 kcal/day (cut deeper) |
| Weight on target but lifts dropping | Add 100–200 kcal/day (mostly carbs) |
| Weight maintaining unintentionally | Recalculate maintenance, restart adjustment |
| Sleep/recovery markers degrading | Refeed week, then reassess |

**One knob per adjustment.** Calories OR macros, not both. If you change two things simultaneously, you can't tell which one moved the needle.

### Macro adjustment order

When cutting calories:
1. **Drop fats first** — easier to maintain protein and training fuel
2. **Then drop carbs** — only after fats are at floor (~0.3 g/lb)
3. **Never drop protein** — stays fixed across phases

When adding calories:
1. **Add carbs first** — anabolic signaling, training fuel
2. **Then fats** — once carbs are sufficient
3. **Protein stays put** — no need to add more once at 0.8–1.0 g/lb

## 9. Hydration & supplements

These are the bottom of the pyramid — they refine, they don't drive results.

**Hydration:**
- Baseline: 1.5 ml of water per kcal eaten (~3000 ml for a 2000-kcal diet)
- Method: drink to thirst + monitor urine color (light yellow = good)
- Pre/intra/post-training: ~500 ml each window for >1h sessions

**High-evidence supplements:**

| Supplement | Dose | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| Creatine monohydrate | 5g/day | Performance + cognition; no downside |
| Whey protein | as-needed | Hit protein target if food alone is hard |
| Caffeine | 200–400mg pre-workout | Performance, hunger suppression during cuts |
| Vitamin D | 2000–4000 IU | Most athletes deficient |
| Omega-3 (EPA/DHA) | 2–3g/day | If fatty fish intake is low |
| Multivitamin | 1/day | Insurance against micronutrient gaps |

**Skip (or evidence-light):**
- BCAAs/EAAs alone — covered if protein target hit
- Glutamine — no real effect outside clinical settings
- Fat burners — mostly caffeine + marketing
- Test boosters — generally bunk

## 10. Coach's notes

- **The 50% number is approximate, not literal.** Calorie balance is ~50% of the variance the diet itself can produce. Genetics, sleep, stress, training quality together account for the other ~50% of body composition outcomes. Don't oversell calorie precision.
- **Set point shifts are slow.** Athletes who've been heavy for years have higher set points; reaching the new equilibrium takes 6+ months of maintenance, not weeks. Manage expectations.
- **Adherence drops at 8–10 weeks.** This is when motivation fades and habit hasn't fully installed. Plan a refeed/break here regardless of progress — it's about the human, not the data.
- **Tracking fatigue is real.** Pure tracking diets fail at month 3–4 not because the math broke but because measurement got tedious. Have an exit plan to looser pattern-following once portions calibrate.
- **Lifts are a better canary than weight.** A scale can stall for water reasons. Lifts dropping means the deficit is biting too hard, full stop.
- **Endurance athletes need different protein.** Israetel's framework is biased toward strength/physique. Pure endurance athletes (marathoners, cyclists) often run 0.6–0.8 g/lb protein and much higher carbs (3–5 g/lb).
- **Female athletes around menstrual cycle.** Water weight swings of 2–4 lb over 7–10 days are normal. Don't react to weekly numbers in isolation — use the rolling average.
- **Physique competition prep is its own beast.** Standard RD principles apply but peaking week (water/sodium manipulation, depletion-supercompensation) requires specialized handling not covered here.

## 11. Programming decision tree

The runbook a nutritionist agent follows to design a block in this style.

### Step 1 — Classify the athlete

- Inputs: profile (bodyweight, training arc, current goal), recent weight trend, lift history
- Decision: which phase fits? (Fat loss / Maintenance / Muscle gain / Long-term maintenance)
- Branch: if cutting → Step 2A; if bulking → Step 2B; if maintaining → Step 2C

### Step 2 — Set caloric target

- Estimate maintenance: bodyweight × activity multiplier (range 14–18 kcal/lb)
- Set deficit/surplus from phase:
  - Fat loss: -300 to -500 kcal
  - Maintenance: ±0 from estimate
  - Muscle gain: +200 to +400 kcal

### Step 3 — Set macros

| Macro | Rule | Calculation |
|---|---|---|
| Protein | 0.8–1.5 g/lb (higher during cuts) | bodyweight × 1.0 g/lb |
| Fat | 0.3–0.5 g/lb minimum | bodyweight × 0.4 g/lb |
| Carbs | Fill remaining calories | (target_kcal − protein_kcal − fat_kcal) ÷ 4 |

### Step 4 — Build meal architecture

- Number of meals: 4–6 (default 5)
- Protein distribution: ~20–25% per meal
- Carb distribution: 60–70% in pre/intra/post-training meals
- Fat distribution: lower in training-window meals; higher elsewhere

### Step 5 — Define adjustment rules

- Weigh-in cadence: 2–3×/week minimum, daily preferred
- Review cadence: every 2–3 weeks (NOT weekly)
- Adjustment: one knob per cycle
- Mandatory refeed: every 8 weeks during cuts

### Step 6 — Define exit criteria

- Fat loss phase: target weight reached OR 12 weeks elapsed → ramp to maintenance
- Muscle gain phase: target weight reached OR fat gain >25% of total weight gain → ramp to maintenance
- Any phase: lifts dropping for 2+ weeks → eat at maintenance until restored

## 12. Common mistakes

1. **Optimizing the wrong layer.** Obsessing over meal timing or supplements while calories are off. Always: hierarchy first.
2. **Adjusting too fast.** Reacting to week-to-week noise. Wait 2–3 weeks of data before changing one knob.
3. **Cutting protein during a cut.** Protein floor is fixed at 1.0–1.5 g/lb. It's the *last* macro to drop, not the first.
4. **No diet break in long cuts.** Running a 17-week cut with no maintenance week. Adherence collapses around weeks 8–10. Insert breaks proactively.
5. **Concurrent recomp ambition.** "I want to lose fat AND build muscle simultaneously." Outside novice/PED contexts, this is much slower than dedicated phases. Pick one direction.
6. **Reading the daily scale.** Body weight fluctuates 2–5 lb daily. The 7-day rolling average is the signal. Don't react to single days.
7. **Cutting through stress periods.** Choosing fat-loss phase during weddings, work crunches, travel, exam season. Adherence requires bandwidth — schedule cuts during low-stress windows.
8. **Ignoring lift trend.** Weight loss looks fine but lifts are dropping for 3 weeks. The cut is failing. Calories go UP regardless of weight.
9. **Generic protein number.** Defaulting to "1g/lb" without considering training stress, deficit depth, or sport. Endurance athletes need less (0.6–0.8); strength athletes in deficit need more (1.2–1.5).
10. **Treating supplements as the lever.** Spending money on creatine/test boosters before fixing the calorie/protein/sleep stack. Pyramid order matters.
11. **No exit ramp from a cut.** Hitting target weight then immediately eating at "normal" again → rebound fat gain. Always ramp through maintenance.
12. **Tracking forever.** Long-term tracking can damage relationship with food. Calibrate during precision phases (4–8 weeks), then switch to pattern-following.

## 13. Style interactions

**Compatible co-styles:**
- Any training style (vertical-jump, dylan-shannon strength, Olympic lifting, mobility) — RD is the meta-framework
- Hybrid athletic styles — RD's training-day classification matches multi-modal training perfectly
- Sport-specific blocks — competition-day nutrition rules layer cleanly on top

**Cooperation rules:**
- **During heavy training blocks:** training-day classification drives caloric and carb periodization. Hard days (>2h, multi-modal) need more carbs and total kcal than light days, even within the same phase.
- **During technique blocks (e.g., Olympic lifting peaking):** maintain calories — no cutting during peaking. Strength preservation is paramount.
- **During mobility-only blocks:** treat as non-training days for caloric purposes. Don't over-eat during rehab phases.
- **During in-season (sport competition):** generally maintenance, with race-day or competition-day adjustments per Chapter 14.

**Calorie/load conflicts:**
- A deep cut (–500 kcal) is incompatible with a peak training block. If the calendar pushes both, the cut yields. Body comp can wait; performance can't.
- Alcohol > 4 drinks/wk is incompatible with serious cutting. Either cut deeper into training or moderate alcohol — can't do both.

**Equipment/facility conflicts:**
- Tracking requires a kitchen scale — ensure athlete has one before starting precision phases
- Whey protein needs cold storage post-training; pack accordingly for travel athletes

**Compatible meta-styles:**
- "If It Fits Your Macros" (IIFYM) — same priority hierarchy, slightly looser on food composition
- Flexible dieting frameworks generally — share the calorie-first thesis

**Incompatible meta-styles:**
- Strict ketogenic (carbs <50g) — conflicts with carbohydrate periodization for high-intensity training
- Carnivore-only — micronutrient deficiencies, fiber gap, fits poorly with athlete needs
- Fasting-heavy (OMAD, prolonged fasts) — protein distribution fails, MPS suboptimal
- "Clean eating" dogma — RD explicitly rejects food-quality moralism in favor of macro hierarchy
