# Dylan Shannon (POWERJACKED) — Training Style Guide

> **Purpose of this guide.** This document is intended to help coaches — human or AI — design programs in the style of Dylan Shannon's POWERJACKED / ATHLETIC methodology. Synthesized from Dylan Shannon's published 6-day training templates, his "7 things for powerful legs" principles, and online research on his programs (powerjacked.com, TrainHeroic). *"Look like a bodybuilder, perform like an athlete."*

A 6-day power-and-physique hybrid style that builds an explosive lower body and a jacked upper body by stacking sprints + plyometrics + Olympic lifts + heavy compounds, then layering high-volume bodybuilding accessories over the top.

---

## 1. When to use this style

**Goal types this serves:**
- Athletes who want to be faster, more explosive, *and* visibly muscular at the same time
- Field-sport athletes (rugby, football, lacrosse, basketball) needing speed + power + size
- Combine prep (bench + sprint + jump emphasis on the same week)
- General-population trainees who want an aesthetic physique without losing athleticism
- Anyone who has plateaued on pure bodybuilding splits and wants to add power expression back in

**Athlete profile fit:**
- Training age: intermediate or above. The session volume (75–90 min lifts, 6 days/week) and exercise variety assume the athlete already has movement competency and recovery capacity.
- Equipment minimum: full barbell + rack + plates + DBs + cable stack + leg press / hack squat / pendulum + bench + open turf or track for sprints + boxes / hurdles for jumps. Effectively a commercial gym + sprint surface.
- Time required: 6 sessions/week. ~4.5–5.5 hours of lifting + 1.5–2 hours of sprint/conditioning. Total ~6–7.5 hr/week.
- Compatible co-styles: General sport practice, vertical-jump (overlapping pillars), powerbuilding.

**Contraindications (do NOT recruit this style if):**
- Athlete cannot recover from 6 sessions/week (poor sleep, large life stress, in-season high-volume sport practice)
- Active lower-body tendinopathy or post-surgical phase < 12 weeks — too much sprint + plyo + heavy hinge volume
- Pure strength-sport peaking (powerlifting meet) — the bodybuilding accessory volume taxes recovery you need for the lifts
- True novice (< 1 year training, no Olympic lift experience) — start with a simpler hypertrophy + linear strength base first; come back to this style after 6–12 months
- Time-constrained athletes < 4 sessions/week — use a different split

> NOTE: contraindications are inferred from training-load principles and Dylan's stated intermediate target audience. Sources do not state them explicitly.

## 2. Mechanism — how this style produces results

Two adaptation streams running in parallel: **(a) explosive force production**, driven by high-CNS, low-volume sprint + plyo + Olympic + heavy compound work scheduled when the nervous system is fresh, and **(b) structural hypertrophy**, driven by moderate-load high-rep accessory work that follows the strength piece in the same session. The sequence matters — putting velocity-based work first preserves quality, while pushing the bodybuilding work to the back end means it can run on the residual fatigue without degrading the high-CNS adaptation.

### 2.1 The four-pillar lower body

Dylan's "7 things for powerful legs" compresses to four operational pillars the program must hit weekly:

| Pillar | Quality trained | Example exercises |
|---|---|---|
| **Sprint maximally** | Top-end speed, RFD | All-out sprints 10–60 yd, 60s rest per 10 yd |
| **Jump high & far** | Reactivity, SSC, vertical/horizontal expression | Continuous CMJs, broad jumps, kneeling jumps, seated jumps, skips, bounds, hops |
| **Move weight violently** | Strength-speed (intent-driven force) | Olympic lifts, weighted/barbell jumps |
| **Squat + hinge heavy** | Maximum force capacity | Back/front/box/hack/Zercher squats, deadlift/RDL/SLDL |

Plus two assistance pillars he calls out specifically:
- **Single-leg strength** — reverse lunges, RFE split squats, walking lunges
- **Hamstring posterior** — GHR, low-back ext, seated/lying curls

If a weekly program doesn't hit all four primary pillars, it's not POWERJACKED — it's some other style.

### 2.2 Push velocity first, then volume

The session ordering is non-negotiable: jumps/sprints/Olympics → heavy compound → moderate compound → bodybuilding accessories. The reason is neural quality. Plyometrics done after a fatiguing squat session are slow plyometrics — useless. Heavy squats done after high-volume hypertrophy work are sub-maximal squats — useless. The first 30–40% of session time is spent on the things that demand a fresh CNS; the remainder is spent on volume work that tolerates fatigue.

### 2.3 Density as a third intensity zone

Dylan periodically uses **time-bounded density sets** — 14-minute clock for back squats with 80% 1RM, 14-min AMRAP OHP at 80%, EMOM deadlifts (12x2 @ 80%). This sits between traditional max-strength and traditional hypertrophy: heavy enough to drive strength, dense enough to drive hypertrophy and conditioning. It's why one program produces both outcomes.

> NOTE: §2.3 is inferred from the recurring presence of density / EMOM / clock sets across sample sessions; Dylan does not name "density" as a labeled mechanism.

### 2.4 Push/pull horizontal vs vertical (upper body)

The two upper days are deliberately split: one horizontal-dominant (bench + barbell row + incline + chest-supported row), one vertical-dominant (chins + dips + incline press + single-arm pulldowns). This balances the joint load and gives twice-weekly frequency to every primary movement pattern without redundant fatigue.

## 3. Athlete assessment

> NOTE: §3 is largely INFERRED. Dylan's published programs do not include an explicit assessment battery in the user's source material, though he has built a "Sport Science Dashboard" tracking VBT, sprint/jump, and wellness — implying he believes in objective monitoring. The tests below are the natural fit for the four-pillar model and what an agent must measure to program intelligently in this style.

**Baseline tests** (run at the start of every block):

| Test | Protocol | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| Back squat 1RM (or e1RM from 3–5RM) | Standard 1RM protocol or AMRAP at 80% with Epley | Max strength capacity (the squat-heavy pillar) |
| Power clean 1RM | Standard 1RM | Strength-speed; sets the % loads for OL work |
| Bench press 1RM | Standard 1RM | Upper push max strength |
| Vertical jump | Best of 3 CMJs from standstill | Reactive / explosive output |
| Broad jump | Best of 3 standstill broad jumps | Horizontal explosive output |
| 20 yd sprint | Best of 3, full recovery | Speed (the sprint-maximally pillar) |
| 40 yd sprint | Best of 2, full recovery | Acceleration + transition speed |

**Classification rules:**

| Profile | Tier | Programming implication |
|---|---|---|
| Squat < 1.25× BW · clean < 0.75× BW · weak jumps | Beginner | Run 4-day version. Cap sprint to 3x20 / 2x30 only. Power clean from blocks or hang to learn the catch. |
| Squat 1.25–1.75× BW · clean 0.75–1.0× BW · solid jumps | Intermediate | The standard 6-day template applies as written. |
| Squat > 1.75× BW · clean > 1.0× BW · strong jumps + sprint | Advanced | Add density sets (14-min squat clock, EMOM DL @ 80%). Layer in barbell jumps at 30–35% squat 1RM. |

**In-block monitoring** (capture every session):
- Top set load on the heaviest compound (squat, DL, bench, OHP)
- Best jump in the session (height or distance) — log it like a lift
- Best sprint time — even one rep at 20 yd, every speed session
- Session RPE (1–10) — used for deload triggers
- Bodyweight (weekly) — calorie / load conflict signal

## 4. Session structure

**Ordering rule:** Velocity → Strength → Volume. Jumps/sprints/Olympics first while fresh. Heavy primary compound second. Secondary compound and accessories last. The order is the program — change it and you change what adaptation you get.

**Session template (lifts — applies to upper or lower):**

1. **Warmup + prep** (5–10 min) — *only on speed days:* switches, power skips, straight-leg bounds, alternating bounds. *On lift days:* movement-specific dynamic prep + ramping warmup sets.
2. **Velocity / Olympic** (8–15 min, 8–20 total reps) — Jumps (seated, kneeling, broad, depth), barbell jumps at 30–35%, power cleans, hang cleans. Low rep, full rest, intent-driven.
3. **Heavy compound** (12–20 min, 3–5 working sets) — Back squat, deadlift, front squat, bench, OHP. Top set + back-off, or density set / EMOM at advanced level.
4. **Secondary compound** (12–20 min, 3–4 sets) — RDL, hack squat, leg press, incline DB press, barbell row, chins/dips. Moderate load, controlled tempo.
5. **Accessories — superset blocks** (15–25 min) — Two superset pairs: typically agonist/antagonist (curl/pushdown, fly/row) or single-joint pump work. Reps 8–15, RPE 7–9.
6. **Calf / direct work** (5 min) — calf raises, ab finisher.

**Speed/conditioning session template:**

1. Warmup + prep (10 min) — switches, skips, bounds (2x10–30 yd each)
2. Sprint pyramid (10–15 min) — e.g., 3x20 yd, 2x30 yd, 2x40 yd, full recovery (60s/10yd)
3. Jumps (5–10 min) — seated/kneeling/broad jumps, 3–4 sets x low reps
4. Loaded velocity (5–10 min) — barbell jumps 3x4 @ 30–35% squat 1RM
5. Olympic finisher (10 min) — power cleans 4x3 @ 70–75% clean 1RM

**Typical duration:**
- Sprints + conditioning: 45–60 min
- Lifts: 75–90 min

**Frequency:**
- 6 sessions / week: 2 sprint-conditioning, 2 lower lifts, 2 upper lifts. One full rest day.
- Recovery: 48h between repeated muscle groups; jump + sprint is on the same day so the rest of the week's CNS load is pre-fatigued together rather than spread.

## 5. Exercise selection

**Primary lifts:**

| Exercise | Purpose in this style | Programming notes |
|---|---|---|
| Back Squat | Cornerstone of the squat-heavy pillar | 3x5 + AMRAP, or 14-min density clock at 80%. 2x/week is acceptable but typically 1x/week heavy + 1x/week as front squat. |
| Front Squat | Quad-dominant max strength + carryover to clean catch | 4x5, 3x6/5/4. Used on the Olympic-lift lower day. |
| Deadlift | Hinge-heavy pillar | Wave loading 4x6,5,4,4 or EMOM 12x2 @ 80%. |
| RDL / Stiff-Legged DL | Hamstring-emphasis hinge | 4x5,5,4,3. Heavier than typical bodybuilding RDLs — Dylan loads them. |
| Power Clean | "Move weight violently" pillar | 4x3 @ 70–75% clean 1RM. Twice/week typical. |
| Bench Press | Horizontal upper push | 3x5 + 1x8–10 back-off. |
| Overhead Press | Vertical upper push (variant: 14-min density set @ 80%) | 3x5 + 1x8–10, or AMRAP density. |
| Chin-up | Vertical upper pull | 3x5 + 1x8–10, weighted at advanced. |
| Barbell Row | Horizontal upper pull | 4x6. |

**Velocity / plyometric inventory:**

| Exercise | Role |
|---|---|
| Seated Jumps | Concentric-only, low SSC demand — good intro plyo, also as warmup for heavier squat day |
| Kneeling Jumps | Hip-extension power, no run-up — minimal joint stress |
| Broad Jumps (single + triple) | Horizontal power |
| Continuous Vertical / Broad Jumps | Reactive SSC at moderate intensity |
| Depth / Hurdle Jumps | High-intensity reactive — only for advanced |
| Power Skips, Bounds, Switches | Sprint-prep + reactive ankle/calf training |
| Barbell Jumps | Loaded power — at 30–35% squat 1RM, 3x4 |

**Allowed accessories (the "look-jacked" half):**
- Leg Extensions (often myo-rep matched 3x16)
- Lying / Seated Hamstring Curls
- DB Bulgarian Split Squat
- Hand-Supported Bulgarian SS
- Hack Squat, Pendulum Squat, Leg Press (often a rest-pause 1x20 to top off the leg day)
- Glute-Ham Raise
- Calf Raises (Westwood, standing) 3x10–20
- DB Incline Bench, T-Bar Row, Chest-Supported Row, Cable Fly, Single-Arm Pulldown, Lat Pulldown
- DB Lateral Raises (5x8–10 — high frequency on upper days)
- Reverse Fly, Cable Upright Row, Cable French Press, Skull Crushers, Preacher Curl, Incline Curl, Tricep Pushdown, Rope Hammer Curl, Rope Pushdown
- Pin Press (seated), Deficit Pushups
- GHD Reverse Crunch (ab finisher)

**Substitution map:**

| If unavailable / contraindicated | Substitute | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Power Clean | Hang power clean → DB clean → KB swing | Preserves intent-driven hip extension when athlete can't catch a barbell |
| Depth / hurdle jumps (knee pain) | Continuous CMJs from floor | Removes the high eccentric stress while keeping the SSC pattern |
| Sprints (no surface) | Bike sprints, hill sprints, sled push at max effort | Preserves max-effort top-end power; loses some specificity |
| Back Squat (back issue) | Front squat or Pendulum / Hack squat | Quad-dominant substitute with less spinal load |
| Hack Squat / Pendulum (no machine) | Heel-elevated front squat or DB Bulgarian SS | Preserves quad emphasis without the machine |

**Disallowed in this style (with reason):**
- Marathon-style running / long zone-2 sessions — directly conflicts with the speed-and-power pillar and adds calorie drain that crushes hypertrophy.
- Daily heavy squatting (Smolov-style) — incompatible with sprint + jump days.
- Pure powerlifting peaking (singles only, no accessories) — strips out the hypertrophy pillar.
- Concurrent CrossFit-style metcons on lift days — degrades CNS quality of subsequent compound lifts.

## 6. Periodization

**Model used:** Block periodization with concurrent qualities — every block runs all four pillars but shifts emphasis week-to-week and across an 8-week cycle.

**Why this model fits this style:** Dylan sells programs as **8-week cycles**. Within a cycle, intensity climbs and accessory volume holds; weeks 7–8 act as a peak/test before reset. Concurrency is forced by the 4-pillar requirement — you can't drop sprints or jumps even in a strength-emphasis week, just shrink them.

**Block structure (typical 8-week cycle):**

| Phase | Weeks | Emphasis | Sprint/Plyo | Heavy Compound | Accessories |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I — Foundation | 1–2 | Build volume tolerance, groove patterns | Med | Med (75–82%) | High |
| II — Strength | 3–4 | Push primary lift loads | Med | High (82–88%) | Med-High |
| III — Power | 5–6 | Express the strength as speed | High | Med-High (80–85%) | Med |
| IV — Realize / test | 7–8 | Density sets, AMRAP top sets, sprint PRs | High (taper) | Test (1RM or AMRAP) | Low (drop a superset) |

**Within-block progression rules:**
- **Add load** on a primary lift when the prescribed top set was completed clean at RPE ≤ 8 the previous week.
- **Hold load** when the top set hit RPE 9 — rep it again the next week before adding.
- **Back off / deload week** when bar speed on velocity work drops > 10% across two sessions, OR sprint times regress two sessions in a row, OR sleep < 6h for 4+ nights. Drop volume 40% for one week before resuming.

**Between-block progression rules:**
- Bump primary lift e1RMs into the new block based on Phase IV test.
- Rotate one heavy compound variant (e.g., back squat → front squat dominant; bench → close-grip dominant).
- Rotate plyo emphasis: jump emphasis (broad/triple broad) → vertical emphasis (CMJ/depth) → loaded emphasis (barbell jumps) across cycles.
- Hold the upper-body bodybuilding accessories largely constant — change rep ranges, not exercises. Hypertrophy compounds reward consistency.

## 8. Technique

> Optional section, kept because Olympic lifts and sprint mechanics materially gate adaptation in this style.

### 8.1 Power Clean

- **Cues:** "Push the floor away," "long arms off the floor," "punch up," "feet flat through the pull, then jump under." Aggressive triple extension, then a fast pull under.
- **Common errors:** Yanking with the arms early (early arm bend kills hip drive); hyperextending the lumbar at the top; soft catch with caved-in knees.
- **Drills:** Hang power clean (teaches the catch), block clean (teaches the second pull), tall muscle clean (teaches the upper pull mechanic).

### 8.2 Sprinting (max effort)

- **Cues:** "Rip the ground back," "knee up, toe up, heel up," "tall posture, no folding at the hips." Full recoveries between reps — 60s per 10 yards is the floor; quality > quantity.
- **Common errors:** Quad-dominant push instead of hip-driven cycling; running sprints fatigued (turns it into conditioning); skipping the prep series (cold sprint = pulled hamstring).
- **Drills:** Single switches → double → triple (teaches cycling mechanics); power skips (teaches projection); straight-leg bounds (teaches stiff-leg landings); alternating bounds (teaches force absorption + reproduction).

### 8.3 Jump (broad / vertical / kneeling)

- **Cues:** "Drive arms hard," "stay tall on the upswing," "land soft, absorb." For broad jumps: lean forward into projection. For verticals: "punch the ceiling." For kneeling jumps: explode hips, don't pre-load knees by drifting backward.
- **Common errors:** Counter-movement too deep on a CMJ (slows it down); landing stiff-legged from broad jumps; rep grinding when the height drops more than ~10%.
- **Drills:** Pause jumps (teaches force at depth), continuous jumps (teaches reactive output), seated jump (teaches concentric-only force).

## 9. Recovery & nutrition

**Style-specific recovery demands:**
- **48h between same-quality CNS sessions.** Sprint Day 1 → Sprint Day 4 (72h) is the spacing pattern. Heavy lower Day 2 → heavy lower Day 5 (72h).
- Sprint + heavy squat back-to-back (e.g., Day 1 sprint → Day 2 heavy lower) is acceptable because the sprint day stresses the CNS and elastic tissues, while the lower day stresses force capacity. But the inverse (heavy squat → sprint next day) tanks sprint quality. Keep the published order.
- **6 sessions/week is the max.** A second consecutive sprint day, or a 7th lift session, is overreaching unless on a deload following.
- **Tendon recovery on plyos.** If patellar / Achilles soreness > 3/10, replace depth jumps with broad jumps for a week.

**Nutritional priorities:**
- Protein **upper-end** (≥1g/lb bodyweight) — the "look jacked" half demands MPS-saturating intake.
- Caloric surplus during Phases I–III if growth is the goal; maintenance or slight surplus during Phase IV testing.
- Carbs around training — especially the speed/conditioning days. Pre + intra carbs preserve sprint quality.
- Avoid extended deficits while running this style. The CNS load + volume punishes under-eating.

**In-season modifications:**
- For an in-season athlete, drop to **4 days**: 1 sprint+conditioning, 1 lower, 1 upper, 1 lighter full-body. Cut accessory volume by ~40%. Preserve the velocity work (jumps, OL singles) at the start of every session.

## 10. Coach's notes

- **Dylan trains himself like he programs.** His own posted sessions (435 x 29 reps in a 14-min back squat clock, EMOM deadlifts at 80%, AMRAP 80% OHP density sets) are the advanced-tier endpoint of the methodology, not the entry point. Don't program a new client this way — they'll break.
- **The accessory volume is what makes this look jacked, not the strength work.** If you cut the supersets and the high-rep finishers to "save recovery," you've taken a power-and-strength program, not POWERJACKED. Either run the full template or call it something else.
- **Density sets are the move when an athlete plateaus.** Not more weight, not more sets. Same weight, time pressure. 14 minutes at 80% will expose recovery gaps and produce both strength and hypertrophy where neither alone was working.
- **The four-pillar test:** before publishing any week, ask "did I sprint, jump, move weight violently, and squat/hinge heavy this week?" If any pillar is missing, fix it before sending the program.
- **Olympic lift quality > Olympic lift load.** Power cleans at 70–75% with sharp execution beat 80%+ sloppy. A common error is athletes inflating clean loads because the program says "x3" — the sets should look fast and clean, not grindy.
- **Single-leg work is non-negotiable on the lower days.** Bulgarian SS (DB or hand-supported) appears almost every lower session. Do not delete it — it's the quad/glute hypertrophy + asymmetry-corrector that distinguishes this style from a generic powerlifting + sprints split.
- **Density-set loading is calibrated to sleep.** If sleep < 6h the night before a 14-min density day, drop to 75% and hit reps as a back-off scheme — don't push the AMRAP. The session is still productive; the test result isn't valid.
- **Watch for "jumping fatigued."** First sign of overreaching in this style: vertical jump drops 1–2 inches across a week. It precedes any drop in squat or sprint by ~5–7 days. Use the daily jump as the leading indicator.

## 11. Programming decision tree

### Step 1 — Classify the athlete
- Inputs: training age, squat 1RM (BW ratio), clean 1RM (BW ratio), VJ, broad jump, 20yd sprint, weekly time available, primary goal (athletic vs aesthetic).
- Decision: 4-day novice / 6-day intermediate / 6-day advanced w/ density. (See §3 classification table.)

### Step 2 — Pick the periodization model variant
- All tiers run an 8-week cycle with the four phases in §6.
- Beginner: skip Phase IV density, replace with a simple 1RM test at week 8.
- Intermediate: standard 8-week cycle as written.
- Advanced: keep the cycle, layer density sets in Phases III–IV.

### Step 3 — Build the session template
- **Lower lift days (2/wk):** Velocity (jumps or OL) → heavy primary squat or hinge → secondary compound → 2 superset blocks → calves.
- **Upper lift days (2/wk):** Heavy primary press or pull → secondary compound → 2 agonist/antagonist supersets → finisher.
- **Sprint+conditioning days (2/wk):** Prep series → sprint pyramid → jumps → loaded velocity (barbell jumps) → Olympic finisher.
- Distribute the 4 pillars: sprint twice (Day 1, 4), jump on every lower + every speed day, OL twice (Day 1 finisher, Day 5 opener), squat heavy twice (alternating front/back), hinge heavy once or twice.

### Step 4 — Set volume and intensity

| Component | Building volume | Maintenance volume |
|---|---|---|
| Sprints | 7–10 reps total per session, 20–40 yd | 4–6 reps, same distance |
| Plyometrics | 20–40 contacts/wk | 10–20 contacts/wk |
| Olympic lifts | 4x3 @ 70–80%, 2x/wk | 3x2 @ 70%, 1x/wk |
| Heavy primary lifts | 3x5 + AMRAP, 1x/wk per lift | 3x3 @ 80% |
| Bodybuilding accessories | 12–20 working sets / muscle group / wk | 8–12 sets |

### Step 5 — Define progression
- **Within-block:** see §6 "within-block progression rules." The trigger to add load is RPE ≤ 8 on the prescribed top set. The trigger to deload is the leading-indicator drop on bar speed or jump height.
- **Between-block:** Phase IV test (week 8) sets new e1RMs for the next cycle. Rotate one squat variant, one OL variant, one upper press variant.

## 12. Common mistakes

1. **Skipping the velocity work to "save the legs."** If you put squats first, you've turned a power program into a strength program. Plyos and OLs must be first or they don't produce the adaptation.
2. **Running the program as 4 days when it's specified as 6.** The pillars don't fit in 4 days at full volume — you have to cut a pillar, and people usually cut sprints or jumps. That's the worst cut. If you only have 4 days, run the 4-DAY POWERJACKED with the deliberate compression pattern, not a hacked-up 6-day.
3. **Treating density sets like a regular AMRAP.** A 14-min squat clock is paced — you do reps, rest 60–120s, do reps, rest. Athletes who go to failure in the first set ruin the rest of the clock and the rest of the week.
4. **Sprinting fatigued.** Sprints after a Tuesday lift = conditioning, not speed work. Sprint days must come into the session with fresh CNS. Reschedule, don't half-effort.
5. **Cutting the bodybuilding accessories.** "Strength athletes don't need that fluff" — that fluff is the visible-muscle outcome of the program. Cut it, and you have a generic powerlifting + plyos block.
6. **Adding load to power cleans by sacrificing speed.** A 75% clean that crawls under is worse than a 70% clean that snaps under. Speed is the adaptation; the % is just a guide.
7. **Running this style on < 6h sleep.** The CNS and volume demands need real recovery. Two weeks of bad sleep + this program = injury or plateau.
8. **No deload at week 8 → straight into another cycle.** The 8-week cycle ends with a test, then a 4–7 day reset. Skipping the reset stacks fatigue across cycles invisibly.
9. **Loading RDLs like a bodybuilder.** Dylan's RDLs are heavy — wave-loaded 4x5,5,4,3. If you're doing 3x12 light, you're not training the hinge pillar, you're doing posterior accessory work.
10. **Mixing this with metcons / CrossFit / running on the same week.** The conditioning is built in. Adding zone-2 or metcons stacks fatigue against the four pillars and slows everything.

## 13. Style interactions

**Day-pairing rules:**
- Sprint day → next day can be a lower lift day (the canonical Day 1 → Day 2 pattern). The reverse — heavy lower then sprint — is forbidden; sprints on cooked legs are useless.
- Heavy hinge (Day 5 deadlift) → at least 48h before next heavy hinge.
- Plyo work + heavy squat in the same session: keep total plyo contacts < 20 and put them all before the squat warmup.

**Recovery overlap:**
- Shares CNS budget heavily with vertical-jump style, sprinting style, Olympic lifting. Don't combine — pick one as the primary, treat this style as the parent.
- Compatible with low-CNS pure hypertrophy or arm/calf isolation work as a secondary style.

**Calorie / load conflicts:**
- High weekly calorie demand (~+500 kcal vs sedentary). Conflicts with aggressive deficit phases — don't run this and a cut > 250 kcal/day deficit simultaneously.
- High glycogen demand (sprints + density sets). Carb-restricted diets (keto, low-carb) are a poor fit.

**Equipment / facility conflicts:**
- Needs full commercial gym + sprint surface (track, turf, parking lot). Home gym athletes must substitute (bike sprints, hill sprints, sled work) and accept some specificity loss.
- Olympic lifting platform is a significant facility constraint — many gyms don't allow drops.

**Compatible co-styles:**
- **Vertical jump** — significant pillar overlap; this style is a generalization of vertical-jump training with bodybuilding added. Pick one as the primary lens; don't program both blocks simultaneously.
- **Powerbuilding** (Dylan's own variant) — natural sister style; trade speed pillar for an additional bench day if pure powerbuilding is the goal.
- **Sport practice** (rugby, football, basketball) — designed for it; reduce sprint+conditioning days when sport practice is high.

**Incompatible co-styles:**
- Endurance/marathon training — direct caloric and adaptation conflict.
- Powerlifting peaking (singles only, low accessory) — strips out the hypertrophy pillar.
- Bodybuilding cut + this style — recovery insufficient; pick a cut-friendly hypertrophy split instead.
